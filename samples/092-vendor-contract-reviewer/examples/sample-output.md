## 🔴 CRITICAL ISSUES

### Issue 1: Asymmetric Cancellation Policy

**What the contract says:**

> *"In the event of cancellation by the Client, the following applies: cancellations made more than ninety (90) days prior to the Event Date shall result in forfeiture of the entire deposit. Cancellations made within ninety (90) days of the Event Date shall result in the Client being responsible for the full contract amount."*

There is no corresponding section describing what happens if the **photographer** cancels.

**What this means in plain English:**

If Priya cancels the wedding for any reason — even if Kendra did something wrong first — she loses at minimum $2,400. If she cancels after June 16, 2024 (90 days before the wedding), she owes the full $4,800 even though she'd receive zero photos. Meanwhile, if Kendra cancels — decides to take a different, higher-paying gig, moves out of state, simply changes her mind — the contract doesn't require her to do anything. No refund obligation. No penalty. No duty to find a replacement.

**Why this is dangerous:**

This is the single most one-sided clause in the contract. Priya bears 100% of the financial risk. In the worst case, Kendra could cancel one week before the wedding, keep $2,400 (or potentially claim the full $4,800), and Priya would have no photographer, no time to book a replacement, and no contractual remedy.

**Industry standard:**

Most reputable wedding photography contracts in Georgia include symmetrical cancellation terms:
- Client cancellation >90 days: forfeiture of deposit (this part is standard)
- Client cancellation <90 days: forfeiture of deposit, possibly a prorated additional fee
- **Photographer cancellation at any time:** full refund of all payments, plus reasonable effort to find a qualified replacement photographer

**Suggested alternative language:**

> *"In the event of cancellation by the Photographer for any reason other than Force Majeure (see §[X]), the Photographer shall (a) refund 100% of all payments received within fourteen (14) business days; and (b) make reasonable best efforts to assist the Client in securing a replacement photographer of comparable experience and style. If the Photographer cancels within sixty (60) days of the Event Date, the Photographer shall additionally pay the Client a cancellation fee of $500 to offset the Client's rebooking costs."*
>
> *"In the event of cancellation by the Client more than ninety (90) days prior to the Event Date, the Client shall forfeit the deposit. In the event of cancellation by the Client within ninety (90) days of the Event Date, the Client shall be responsible for the deposit plus 25% of the remaining balance, provided the Photographer has not yet turned away other bookings for the Event Date."*

**Negotiation script:**

> "Kendra, I'm really excited about working with you — your portfolio is exactly what I've been looking for. I did want to talk through the cancellation section. Right now, it covers what happens if I cancel, which I totally understand. But I noticed it doesn't say what happens if something comes up on your end. I'm not expecting that at all! But since we're putting this in writing, I'd feel a lot better if we had something symmetrical — so we're both protected. Would you be open to adding a clause that guarantees a full refund if you need to cancel, plus maybe helping me find a replacement? I think that's pretty standard in the industry."

### Issue 2: No Backup Photographer Clause

**What the contract says:**

Nothing. The contract does not address what happens if Kendra Davis is unable to perform on the wedding day due to illness, injury, family emergency, or any other reason.

**What this means in plain English:**

If Kendra breaks her leg the morning of September 14, Priya has no contractual guarantee that anyone will show up to photograph her wedding. There is no obligation for Kendra to find a substitute, no timeline for notification, and no automatic refund if she simply doesn't appear.

**Why this is dangerous:**

Wedding days cannot be rescheduled to accommodate a vendor's emergency. Every other element of the day — the venue, the caterer, the florist, the officiant, the guests' travel — is locked in. Photography is one of the only vendors whose work product cannot be recreated after the fact. A missing photographer means those moments are gone forever.

**Industry standard:**

Professional wedding photography contracts routinely include a backup/substitute clause:
- Photographer will make reasonable efforts to secure a qualified substitute
- Substitute must be of comparable skill and style
- Client must be notified as soon as reasonably possible
- If no substitute can be found, full refund of all payments

**Suggested alternative language:**

> *"In the event the Photographer is unable to perform services on the Event Date due to illness, injury, emergency, or other cause beyond the Photographer's reasonable control, the Photographer shall: (a) notify the Client as soon as practicable; (b) make reasonable best efforts to secure a substitute photographer of comparable skill, style, and experience, subject to the Client's approval, which shall not be unreasonably withheld; and (c) if no mutually agreeable substitute can be secured, refund 100% of all payments received within fourteen (14) business days. The use of a substitute photographer shall not alter any other terms of this Agreement."*

**Negotiation script:**

> "One more thing I wanted to bring up — I know this is unlikely, but the contract doesn't mention what would happen if you got sick or had an emergency on the wedding day. I've read that it's pretty common to have a backup clause, just so we both know the plan. Would you be open to adding something that says you'd try to find a qualified replacement, and if that's not possible, we'd get a full refund? I think it would give us both peace of mind."

### Issue 3: Vague Image Delivery Timeline

**What the contract says:**

> *"Edited images will be delivered to the Client via online gallery within a reasonable timeframe following the Event Date."*

**What this means in plain English:**

"Reasonable timeframe" is not defined. It could mean 4 weeks or 9 months. Priya has no contractual basis to demand delivery by any specific date. If she wants to send thank-you cards with wedding photos, create an album for the holidays, or simply see her wedding pictures before her first anniversary, the contract gives her no leverage.

**Why this is dangerous:**

This is one of the most common complaints in the wedding photography industry. Without a deadline, photographers who overbook themselves can push delivery out indefinitely. Priya's only recourse would be to send politely worded emails and hope for the best. If she withholds final payment as leverage, the photographer could argue breach of contract.

**Industry standard:**

Atlanta-area wedding photographers in this price range typically commit to:
- Sneak peek (10–20 images) within 1–2 weeks
- Full gallery delivery within 6–10 weeks
- Some contracts allow up to 12 weeks with a written notice provision

**Suggested alternative language:**

> *"The Photographer shall deliver a preview gallery of no fewer than fifteen (15) edited images within fourteen (14) days of the Event Date. The complete gallery of all edited images shall be delivered via online gallery within eight (8) weeks of the Event Date. If the Photographer anticipates a delay, the Photographer shall notify the Client in writing at least seven (7) days before the applicable deadline and provide a revised delivery date no more than three (3) weeks beyond the original deadline. If the complete gallery is not delivered within twelve (12) weeks of the Event Date, the Client shall be entitled to a 10% discount on the contract price for each additional week of delay, up to a maximum of 30%."*

**Negotiation script:**

> "I also wanted to ask about the image delivery timeline. The contract says 'reasonable timeframe,' and I totally trust your workflow, but I'd love to have something specific in writing — partly so I can plan for things like thank-you cards and albums. Would you be comfortable committing to a sneak peek within two weeks and the full gallery within eight weeks? And if something comes up, maybe just a written heads-up? I think that's pretty in line with what most photographers do."

### Issue 4: Overly Restrictive Copyright and Licensing

**What the contract says:**

> *"The Photographer shall retain all rights, title, and interest in and to the Images, including but not limited to copyright. The Client is granted a limited, non-exclusive, non-transferable personal use license to display, share, and reproduce the Images for personal, non-commercial purposes. Any reproduction by a third-party printing service requires prior written authorization from the Photographer."*

**What this means in plain English:**

Priya is paying $4,800 but will not own her wedding photos. She can post them on social media and display them at home, but she cannot walk into Costco, CVS, or Shutterfly and order prints without first getting written permission from Kendra. She cannot submit a photo to a magazine or blog. She cannot use them on a personal website if it has any commercial element (like a link to her business). If she wants a canvas print from a third-party vendor, she needs to email Kendra and wait for approval — for every single order, potentially for the rest of her life.

**Industry standard:**

While photographers typically retain copyright (this is standard and legal under U.S. copyright law), most wedding photographers in the $4,000+ range grant a **broad personal use license** that explicitly permits:
- Printing at any lab or service without additional permission
- Sharing on social media with credit
- Personal use on websites, invitations, and announcements
- Submitting to wedding blogs/publications (with photographer credit)

Some photographers in higher price ranges offer full copyright transfer for an additional fee.

**Suggested alternative language:**

> *"The Photographer retains copyright to all Images. The Client is granted a perpetual, non-exclusive, worldwide license to reproduce, display, distribute, and create derivative works from the Images for any personal, non-commercial purpose, including but not limited to: printing at any photo lab or printing service; sharing on social media, personal websites, and blogs; and submitting to publications for editorial (non-advertising) use, provided the Photographer is credited where reasonably practical. This license is transferable to the Client's immediate family members. The Client may not sell the Images, sublicense them for commercial use, or use them in paid advertising without the Photographer's written consent."*

**Negotiation script:**

> "I noticed the licensing section requires written permission for third-party printing. I totally respect your copyright — I'm not looking to sell the photos or anything like that. But I'd love to be able to order prints from wherever is most convenient without needing to reach out each time. Could we adjust the license to allow personal printing at any lab? I'm happy to credit you whenever I share online."

### Issue 5: No Force Majeure Clause

**What the contract says:**

Nothing. There is no provision addressing events beyond either party's control.

**What this means in plain English:**

If a hurricane hits Atlanta the week of the wedding and The Foundry at Puritan Mill is damaged, if there's a public health emergency and gatherings are banned, if there's a power outage across the city, or if any other extraordinary event prevents the wedding from happening — the contract has no mechanism for handling it. Under the current cancellation terms, Priya could still owe the full $4,800 even if the wedding is cancelled due to a natural disaster.

**Industry standard:**

Force majeure clauses have become near-universal in event contracts since 2020. Standard language covers: natural disasters, pandemics, government orders, venue closures, severe weather, civil unrest, and utility failures. Typical remedies include date rescheduling at no extra cost or pro-rata refund.

**Suggested alternative language:**

> *"Neither party shall be liable for failure to perform obligations under this Agreement due to causes beyond their reasonable control, including but not limited to: natural disasters, pandemics, epidemics, government orders or restrictions, acts of terrorism, severe weather events, venue closures, or widespread utility failures ('Force Majeure Event'). In the event of a Force Majeure Event that prevents the Event from occurring on the scheduled Event Date, the parties shall work in good faith to reschedule the Event within twelve (12) months of the original Event Date at no additional cost, subject to the Photographer's availability. If the parties cannot agree on a rescheduled date within sixty (60) days, the Client shall receive a refund of all payments less a 10% administrative fee."*

**Negotiation script:**

> "One thing I didn't see in the contract is a force majeure clause — basically what happens if something totally outside our control prevents the wedding from happening, like severe weather or a venue emergency. After everything that happened with COVID, I think it's smart for both of us to have a plan. Could we add something that lets us reschedule within a year at no extra cost, or get most of our money back if rescheduling isn't possible?"

### Issue 6: Excessive Overtime Rate and Full-Hour Billing

**What the contract says:**

> *"Coverage beyond the contracted eight (8) hours shall be billed at a rate of $500 per hour, charged in full-hour increments. Overtime charges shall be invoiced within seven (7) business days following the Event Date."*

**What this means in plain English:**

$500/hour is $83 per minute. If Priya's reception runs 10 minutes over and she wants Kendra to stay, that's $500. Full-hour increments mean there is no pro-rating — 1 minute over the 8-hour mark costs the same as 60 minutes over. At $4,800 for 8 hours, the base rate is $600/hour, so the overtime rate of $500/hour is actually slightly below the base rate on a per-hour basis. However, the full-hour increment billing is where the cost becomes unreasonable.

**Industry standard:**

- Overtime rates in the Atlanta market: $300–$600/hour
- Billing increments: Most photographers bill in 30-minute increments; some bill in 15-minute increments
- Some photographers include a 30-minute grace period

**Suggested alternative language:**

> *"Coverage beyond the contracted eight (8) hours shall be billed at a rate of $400 per hour, charged in thirty (30) minute increments (i.e., $200 per half hour). A grace period of fifteen (15) minutes shall apply before overtime charges begin. Overtime must be mutually agreed upon in advance, either in writing or verbally with written confirmation within 24 hours. The Client shall not be charged for overtime that was not explicitly authorized."*

**Negotiation script:**

> "The overtime section is pretty straightforward, but I wanted to ask about two things. First, would you be open to billing in half-hour increments instead of full hours? I'd hate for a 15-minute overrun to cost us $500. And second, could we add a small grace period — maybe 15 minutes — before overtime kicks in? I think that's pretty common and it would help us both avoid an awkward clock-watching situation at the reception."

### Issue 7: Mandatory Arbitration in Cobb County

**What the contract says:**

> *"Any disputes arising under or in connection with this Agreement shall be resolved through binding arbitration conducted in Cobb County, Georgia, in accordance with the rules of the American Arbitration Association. Each party shall bear its own costs and attorneys' fees."*

**What this means in plain English:**

If anything goes wrong — Kendra doesn't deliver photos, delivers poor quality, cancels last-minute — Priya cannot sue in court. She must go through arbitration, and that arbitration must take place in Cobb County, which is where Kendra's business is based. Priya lives in Fulton County. While Cobb County isn't far (about 30 minutes depending on traffic), the principle matters: the contract forces disputes onto the photographer's home turf. Additionally, each party bears its own costs, meaning even if Priya wins, she can't recover her legal fees.

**Industry standard:**

Many small business contracts do include arbitration clauses, which isn't inherently problematic. However, best practice is to specify a neutral location or allow the filing party to choose. Some consumer-friendly contracts offer mediation as a first step before arbitration.

**Suggested alternative language:**

> *"The parties agree to attempt to resolve any dispute arising under this Agreement through good-faith negotiation. If negotiation is unsuccessful within thirty (30) days, the parties agree to non-binding mediation in Fulton County, Georgia, with a mutually agreed-upon mediator. If mediation does not resolve the dispute, either party may pursue binding arbitration in Fulton County, Georgia, or such other location as the parties mutually agree. The prevailing party in any arbitration shall be entitled to recover reasonable attorneys' fees and costs."*

**Negotiation script:**

> "I noticed the dispute resolution section specifies Cobb County for arbitration. I'm hoping we never need it, but since I'm based in Fulton County, would you be open to either meeting in the middle or using Fulton County? Also, could we add a mediation step first? It's cheaper and faster for both of us, and it usually resolves things without needing formal arbitration."

### Issue 9: Vendor Meal Clause Without Remedy

**What the contract says:**

> *"The Client shall provide a vendor meal for the Photographer and second shooter during the Event."*

### Order of Asks

**Start with the easy wins.** Build goodwill before raising harder issues.

1. **Delivery timeline** — easiest yes, frames you as organized not adversarial
2. **Backup photographer clause** — another easy yes, shows you're thinking practically
3. **Force majeure** — easy post-COVID, frames as mutual protection
4. **Symmetrical cancellation** — the big one, but positioned after several agreements
5. **Copyright/printing license** — medium difficulty, high everyday impact
6. **Overtime increments** — reasonable, saves money on edge cases
7. **Second shooter guarantee** — small ask, easy to package with others
8. **Arbitration location** — raise only if Kendra has been receptive overall
9. **Meal clause** — mention casually, don't spend negotiation capital on it

### Tone Guidance

Kendra is a small business owner and an artist. She wrote this contract (or had a lawyer write it) to protect herself, which is understandable. The goal is not to make her feel attacked or accused of being shady. Frame every request as:
- "I want to protect **both** of us"
- "I've heard this is **pretty standard** in the industry"
- "I'm really excited to work with you and just want to make sure we **start off on the same page**"

Avoid: legal threats, ultimatums, comparisons to specific competitors, or implying she wrote the contract in bad faith.
