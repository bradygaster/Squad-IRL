## Final Output

# Artist Statement - Elena Rodriguez

I grew up in Youngstown, Ohio, in the shadow of a steel mill that closed when I was eight. The skeleton of that factory—rusted I-beams, coiled wire, shattered windows—became my playground. My dad, who'd worked there as a welder before the layoffs, would take me on walks through the industrial graveyard and point out the beauty in the decay: the way rust blooms in burnt orange fractals, how sheet metal warps and folds like fabric, the unexpected geometry of collapsed structures.

I make sculptures from the same materials that built (and broke) the Rust Belt: industrial steel, copper wire, scavenged factory hardware. I source most of my materials from scrapyards in the Midwest—the places where dead industries go to be dismantled. I'm drawn to objects that have already lived a life: metal that's been bent by machinery, corroded by weather, shaped by failure. Each piece carries a history of labor, use, and abandonment.

In my work, I don't clean up or "upcycle" these materials in the trendy sense. I'm not interested in making industrial waste palatable or decorative. Instead, I build skeletal structures—often 5 to 7 feet tall—that embrace the imperfections: the rust, the dents, the jagged edges. I weld fragments together using the same techniques my father taught me, leaving the seams visible, letting the burn marks show. The sculptures don't try to hide their origins; they amplify them.

The forms I create are abstract, but they reference architectural elements—beams, trusses, scaffolding—that once held up factories, warehouses, and bridges. Structures built to last forever that were abandoned in a generation. I'm interested in that broken promise: the idea that we build monuments to productivity and progress, then walk away when they're no longer profitable. What's left behind isn't just metal; it's the ghost of a community, a way of life, thousands of livelihoods.

People often ask if my work is about environmentalism or anti-capitalism, and honestly, it's simpler than that. It's about paying attention to what we discard. It's about recognizing beauty in things we've decided are ugly or worthless. It's about the fact that my dad, who spent 30 years shaping metal, now drives past the ruins of his former workplace and sees nothing but failure. I want him—and anyone who walks through their own Rust Belt town—to see something else: resilience, transformation, a kind of haunted dignity.

My current series, *Afterlives*, consists of freestanding sculptures installed in industrial or post-industrial spaces (warehouses, factory floors, urban lots). The work is site-specific in spirit, even when moved to a gallery—it needs space to breathe, to cast shadows, to feel like it belongs to a larger infrastructure. I light them dramatically, so the rust glows amber and the negative space becomes as important as the material itself. Viewers are invited to walk around and through the pieces, to experience them as they would architecture: from multiple angles, noticing how the forms shift as they move.

I'm not trying to memorialize the past or offer easy nostalgia. I'm asking: What do we owe to the things—and the people—we've left behind? What becomes of beauty when it's no longer useful? And how do we hold space for both grief and possibility in the same rusted frame?
