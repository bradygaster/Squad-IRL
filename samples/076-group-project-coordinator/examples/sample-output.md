# Group Project Coordinator — Example Output

> This is what the agent team produces when you run this sample.

## Scenario

Five students in Professor Whitfield's MKT 4450: Marketing Strategy course at the University of Central Florida must deliver a **30-page competitive analysis report** and a **15-minute presentation** for the athletic footwear brand **On Running** (publicly traded as ONON). The project is worth 35% of their final grade and is due **Friday, April 18, 2025**. The team members are:

- **Aisha Patel** — Team lead. Junior, Marketing major, strong writer. Available ~25 hrs/week for project work.
- **Jordan Reeves** — Senior, Marketing & Data Analytics double major. Excellent with data visualization and Excel. Available ~22 hrs/week.
- **Maya Sandoval** — Junior, Marketing major, minor in Graphic Design. Strong at slide design and visual storytelling. Available ~20 hrs/week.
- **Tyler Brooks** — Senior, Marketing major. Works 25 hrs/week at a Best Buy retail job (Tuesday/Thursday/Saturday shifts). Strong presenter and public speaker. Available ~12 hrs/week for project work.
- **Priya Chakraborty** — Junior, Marketing major. Transfer student, first semester at UCF. Eager but less experienced with competitive analysis frameworks. Available ~22 hrs/week.

The team had their first meeting on Monday, March 17, 2025. They have 4 weeks and 4 days until the deadline.

## What the Agents Did

### Round 1: First Draft

**ProjectManager** produced:

A project plan that divided the report into five sections and assigned one section per person: Aisha takes the Executive Summary and Company Overview, Jordan handles Financial Analysis, Maya does Competitor Profiles, Tyler covers Market Trends, and Priya writes the SWOT and Recommendations. Each person was given a single deadline of April 14 (4 days before the due date) to submit their section. A brief meeting schedule of "meet every Monday" was included. The plan estimated the project at about 100 total hours but did not break this down by person or task.

**FairnessJudge** scored it **66/100** and found:

- **Severely unequal workload**: Aisha's assignment (Executive Summary + Company Overview) is ~25 hours of work, while Tyler's (Market Trends) is ~15 hours — but Tyler has the LEAST availability (12 hrs/week). Meanwhile, Priya's SWOT + Recommendations section is the most complex and she's the least experienced team member with no support plan
- **No dependency mapping**: The Executive Summary literally cannot be written until all other sections are done, yet Aisha is assigned to write it alongside (not after) other sections. The Recommendations section depends on all analysis being complete. These dependencies aren't acknowledged
- **No communication plan**: "Meet every Monday" is insufficient — no agenda structure, no async communication channel, no check-in cadence between meetings, no decision-making process
- **No contingency planning**: Tyler works retail with unpredictable schedule changes. What happens if he gets extra shifts? What if someone gets sick during finals prep? No backup plan exists
- **Single deadline is dangerous**: Having everyone submit their final sections 4 days before the due date leaves no time for integration, consistency editing, or presentation rehearsal
- **Priya needs support**: As a transfer student doing her first competitive analysis, assigning her the most strategic section (SWOT + Recommendations) without mentorship or review checkpoints sets her up to struggle

### Round 2: Improved Draft

**CommunicationHub** addressed the feedback:

The project plan was restructured around a 4-week Gantt-style timeline with weekly milestones and clear dependencies. Task assignments were rebalanced to account for Tyler's limited availability (lighter writing load, leveraged for presentation prep where his skills shine) and Priya's learning curve (paired with Jordan for analytical sections, with intermediate review points). A comprehensive communication plan was added including a Discord server structure, twice-weekly check-ins, and a decision-making protocol. Contingency plans were created for common failure scenarios including schedule conflicts, illness, and scope creep. The workload was rebalanced to approximately 42-48 hours per person over the 4-week period, with Tyler at 38 hours accounting for his constraints.

**FairnessJudge** scored it **94/100**: "Meets quality threshold."

## Contingency Plans

### Scenario 1: Tyler Gets Extra Shifts
**Trigger**: Tyler's Best Buy manager schedules additional shifts beyond Tue/Thu/Sat, reducing his availability below 10 hrs/week.
**Plan**:
- Tyler immediately posts in #blockers with his updated schedule
- His research tasks (3.3: Adidas/NB profiles) can be redistributed to Priya (who has capacity in Week 1)
- His presentation tasks are harder to reassign — if he loses availability in Week 5, the team shifts Rehearsal #1 to a time that works, or Tyler records his sections and gets feedback asynchronously
- **Absolute minimum Tyler commitment**: Attend both rehearsals and present his slides on April 18. All other tasks can be redistributed if needed.

### Scenario 2: A Team Member Gets Sick
**Trigger**: Any member is unable to work for 3+ consecutive days.
**Plan**:
- Post in #blockers immediately with expected return date
- If a **writer** is sick: Their reviewer takes over writing, and another member picks up the review
- If a **reviewer** is sick: Another member reviews (anyone can review)
- If **Aisha** is sick during Week 5 (critical — she writes Executive Summary and does final edit): Jordan assumes lead writer duties; Maya handles the merge/edit
- If **Maya** is sick during Week 5 (critical — she designs slides and merges the report): Aisha does the merge in Google Docs (less polished but functional); Tyler handles basic slide formatting using a template Maya pre-creates in Week 3 as a backup
- **Backup slide template**: Maya creates a basic branded template by March 28 (Task 7.0 — added to her Week 2 tasks, 1 hour)

### Scenario 3: Major Scope Creep / Professor Changes Requirements
**Trigger**: Professor Whitfield adds or changes requirements mid-project.
**Plan**:
- Aisha emails Professor Whitfield within 24 hours to clarify expectations
- Emergency Monday meeting (or sooner) to assess impact
- If the change adds pages: Cut depth in non-critical sections rather than adding hours
- If the change alters structure: Re-map dependencies, adjust timeline, communicate new deadlines within 48 hours

### Scenario 4: Merge Conflicts / Inconsistent Tone
**Trigger**: Sections written by different people sound disjointed when combined.
**Plan**:
- This is EXPECTED — it's why Aisha's final edit (Task 8.2) exists
- Mitigation: All writers use the same style guide (APA 7th, 12pt Times New Roman, formal academic tone, third person)
- Aisha creates a **1-page style guide** by March 19 covering: voice, tense, heading format, citation style, how to reference data/figures
- Jordan creates a **shared data glossary** by March 24 — key metrics, company names, abbreviations — so everyone uses consistent terminology

### Scenario 5: Team Conflict
**Trigger**: Two members disagree on an approach, or one member feels the workload is unfair.
**Plan**:
- First: Discuss in Monday meeting openly
- If unresolved: Aisha mediates a private conversation with the two parties
- If still unresolved: Escalate to Professor Whitfield's office hours (Wednesday 2-4 PM)
- **Preemptive**: Weekly Monday meetings include a 1-minute anonymous "temperature check" (everyone holds up 1-5 fingers for "how's the team dynamic?") — if anyone shows 2 or below, Aisha follows up privately
