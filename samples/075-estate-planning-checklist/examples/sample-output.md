## Scenario

Robert and Linda Torres are both 67 years old, married for 40 years, and living in Coral Springs, Florida. They have two adult children — their son Marcus (39, married, lives in Tampa, works as a civil engineer) and their daughter Elena (35, single, lives in Miami, works as a pediatric nurse). Marcus and his wife Daniela have one child, 4-year-old Sofia (the Torres' only grandchild). Robert retired two years ago from a career in logistics management; Linda still works part-time as a bookkeeper. They own their primary residence (estimated value $485,000, remaining mortgage $62,000) and a small rental property — a duplex in Pompano Beach (estimated value $340,000, fully paid off, generating $2,800/month in rental income). They have no existing estate plan: no will, no trust, no power of attorney documents, and no healthcare directives. Robert's recent health scare (a mild cardiac event in January 2025) prompted them to finally address this.

## Section 7: Key Florida-Specific Notes

1. **Homestead Protection**: Florida's homestead exemption protects your primary residence from most creditors (unlimited value, up to half-acre in a municipality). This is one of the strongest homestead protections in the nation. However, you cannot freely devise (leave by will) the homestead if you're married — the surviving spouse has rights to the property regardless of what the will says.

2. **No State Estate or Inheritance Tax**: Florida does not impose a state-level estate tax or inheritance tax. The federal estate tax exemption is $13.61 million per individual in 2025 ($27.22 million for a married couple with portability). The Torres estate is well below this threshold.

3. **Will Execution Requirements**: A Florida will must be signed by the testator in the presence of two attesting witnesses, who must also sign in the presence of the testator and each other (§732.502). A self-proving affidavit (notarized) allows the will to be admitted to probate without witness testimony.

4. **Probate in Florida**: Florida has formal and summary probate administration. Summary administration is available for estates under $75,000 (excluding homestead). The Torres estate will require formal administration unless assets are structured to avoid probate (via trust, beneficiary designations, or TOD/POD).

5. **Elective Share**: A surviving spouse in Florida is entitled to 30% of the "elective estate" regardless of what the will or trust says (§732.2065). This is an important consideration in planning.

6. **Lady Bird Deed**: Florida recognizes Enhanced Life Estate Deeds ("Lady Bird Deeds"), which allow property to pass to beneficiaries at death without probate while retaining full control during life. This could be useful for the rental property if a trust is not established.

7. **Designation of Health Care Surrogate**: Florida uses a specific statutory form for healthcare surrogate designation (§765.202), which is different from a general "healthcare power of attorney" used in other states. Make sure to use the Florida-specific form.

8. **Tangible Personal Property Memorandum**: Florida allows a separate written statement (referenced in the will) that distributes tangible personal property to specific people (§732.515). This is useful for items like Linda's mother's ring and Robert's watch collection — it can be changed without re-executing the will.
