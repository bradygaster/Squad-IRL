# 📜 Unit Plan: The U.S. Constitution — Foundation of American Government

**Teacher:** Ms. Angela Torres
**School:** Jefferson Middle School, Richmond, VA
**Grade/Course:** 8th Grade Civics
**Duration:** 5 days (50 minutes each)
**Class Size:** 28 students
**Standards:** Virginia SOL CE.2a–d, CE.6a–c

---

## Unit Overview

**Essential Question:** *Why does the Constitution still matter 237 years after it was written, and how does its design affect your life today?*

**Unit Objectives — Students Will Be Able To (SWBAT):**
1. Explain the purposes of government as stated in the Preamble (CE.2a)
2. Identify the structure of the Constitution (articles, amendments, Preamble) (CE.2b)
3. Describe the principles of government: consent of the governed, limited government, rule of law, democracy, representative government (CE.2c)
4. Analyze how the separation of powers and checks and balances prevent tyranny (CE.6a–c)
5. Apply constitutional principles to modern scenarios

**Assessment Strategy:**

| Type | When | What |
|------|------|------|
| Formative | Daily | Exit tickets (3-5 min) |
| Formative | Day 3 | Mid-unit check: Padlet response |
| Summative | Day 5 | Unit assessment (test OR project option) |

**Differentiation Key:**
- 🟢 = On-grade level
- 🔵 = Advanced extension
- 🟡 = IEP accommodations (extended time, modified text, graphic organizers)

---

## Day 1: Why Do We Need a Constitution? (Monday)

**SOL Focus:** CE.2a — Purposes of government
**Learning Objective:** SWBAT explain at least 3 purposes of government as stated in the Preamble using textual evidence.
**Bloom's Level:** Understand → Analyze

### Lesson Flow (50 min)

| Time | Activity | Details |
|------|----------|---------|
| 0–5 min | **Bell Ringer** | Project the Preamble on the board (no context). Students write: "What do you think this is? What does it mean in your own words?" |
| 5–15 min | **Vocabulary Front-Loading** | 8 key terms on slides with student-friendly definitions: *Preamble, domestic tranquility, posterity, ordain, establish, welfare, liberty, justice*. Students create a 4-square vocab card (term, definition, picture, sentence) for 4 terms. |
| 15–30 min | **Close Reading: The Preamble** | Distribute Preamble text (annotated version for 🟡 students with built-in glossary). Students highlight and annotate: Blue = purpose of government, Red = who has the power. Turn and talk: "According to this, where does the government get its power?" |
| 30–42 min | **Modern Connections Activity** | Groups of 4 receive a set of 6 scenario cards (e.g., "The town builds a new public library," "Police arrest a robbery suspect," "Congress declares war"). Groups sort cards by which Preamble purpose they fulfill. 🔵 Advanced: Groups create 2 additional scenario cards for classmates. 🟡 IEP: Provide a reference sheet with Preamble purposes matched to simple definitions. |
| 42–47 min | **Share Out** | 2–3 groups share their most debated scenario. Class votes on correct Preamble purpose. |
| 47–50 min | **Exit Ticket** | "Name 2 purposes of government from the Preamble and give a modern example of each." (🟡 Modified: fill-in-the-blank version) |

**Materials:** Preamble handout (2 versions), scenario cards (printed), vocab 4-square template, exit ticket slips

---

## Day 2: The Architecture of the Constitution (Tuesday)

**SOL Focus:** CE.2b — Structure of the Constitution; CE.6a — Separation of powers
**Learning Objective:** SWBAT diagram the structure of the Constitution and explain how power is divided among three branches.
**Bloom's Level:** Understand → Apply

### Lesson Flow (50 min)

| Time | Activity | Details |
|------|----------|---------|
| 0–5 min | **Bell Ringer** | "If you were designing a government from scratch, how would you prevent one person from having too much power?" Quick-write, 2 min. Share with elbow partner. |
| 5–15 min | **Mini-Lecture: Blueprint of the Constitution** | Slides covering: Preamble → 7 Articles → 27 Amendments. Focus on Articles I (Legislative), II (Executive), III (Judicial). Use a "house" metaphor: Preamble = foundation, Articles = rooms, Amendments = renovations. |
| 15–35 min | **Constitution Scavenger Hunt (Chromebooks)** | Students use the National Constitution Center's interactive Constitution (constitutioncenter.org/interactive-constitution). Worksheet with 12 questions requiring students to find specific information in the actual text. Examples: "Which article describes the powers of Congress?" "How old must a President be?" "Which amendment abolished slavery?" 🟡 IEP: Reduced to 8 questions with article/section hints provided. 🔵 Advanced: 4 bonus questions requiring cross-referencing multiple articles. |
| 35–43 min | **Build-a-Government Diagram** | Students create a visual diagram showing 3 branches with key powers. Can use paper or Google Drawing on Chromebook. |
| 43–47 min | **Gallery Walk** (brief) | Students walk past 4–5 displayed diagrams and leave one post-it note comment or question on each. |
| 47–50 min | **Exit Ticket** | "Name the 3 branches, their article number, and one power each holds." |

**Materials:** Chromebooks (class set), scavenger hunt worksheet, diagram template, post-it notes

---

## Day 3: Checks and Balances — The Power Puzzle (Wednesday)

**SOL Focus:** CE.6b–c — Checks and balances, separation of powers
**Learning Objective:** SWBAT analyze how the system of checks and balances prevents any one branch from becoming too powerful.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze → Evaluate

### Lesson Flow (50 min)

| Time | Activity | Details |
|------|----------|---------|
| 0–5 min | **Bell Ringer** | Show a political cartoon depicting checks and balances (e.g., a three-legged stool). "What is this cartoon saying about how our government works?" |
| 5–18 min | **Checks & Balances Simulation** | Role-play activity. Divide class into 3 groups: Congress, President, Supreme Court. Present 3 scenarios and have each branch respond: **Scenario 1:** Congress passes a law banning all social media for minors. What can the President do? What can the Court do? **Scenario 2:** The President signs an executive order giving themselves a 4th term. What can Congress do? What can the Court do? **Scenario 3:** The Supreme Court rules that schools can't teach history. What can Congress do? What can the President do? |
| 18–30 min | **Checks & Balances Map** | Using Chromebooks, students contribute to a class **Padlet** board. Each student posts at least one check and balance with an explanation. Example: "The President can veto a law passed by Congress — this prevents Congress from passing laws the executive branch thinks are harmful." **Mid-unit formative check:** Teacher reviews Padlet in real-time to identify misconceptions. 🟡 IEP: Provide a word bank of checks and balances; student matches and explains one. 🔵 Advanced: Students find a real historical example for their check (e.g., "In 2014, the Supreme Court ruled in NLRB v. Noel Canning that President Obama's recess appointments were unconstitutional"). |
| 30–42 min | **"What If?" Analysis** | Groups receive a "What If?" card: "What if there were no checks and balances? What would happen if the President could make laws AND judge whether they're constitutional?" Groups write a 1-paragraph response and share out. This directly addresses CE.6c (why separation of powers matters). |
| 42–47 min | **Class Discussion** | "Which branch do you think has the most power today? Why?" (No right answer — designed to spark debate and deeper thinking.) |
| 47–50 min | **Exit Ticket** | "Describe one check that the judicial branch has over the legislative branch, and explain why this check is important." |

**Materials:** Political cartoon (projected), scenario cards, Chromebooks, Padlet board (pre-created), "What If?" cards

---

## Day 4: The Constitution in Your Life (Thursday)

**SOL Focus:** CE.2c–d — Principles of government applied to contemporary issues
**Learning Objective:** SWBAT evaluate how constitutional principles affect real-life situations and current events.
**Bloom's Level:** Apply → Create

### Lesson Flow (50 min)

| Time | Activity | Details |
|------|----------|---------|
| 0–5 min | **Bell Ringer** | "Has the government ever affected YOUR life? Think of one example." (Think-pair-share) |
| 5–15 min | **Principles Review Blitz** | Quick-fire review of 5 principles using **Quizizz** on Chromebooks (10 questions, 45 seconds each): consent of the governed, limited government, rule of law, democracy, representative government. Teacher uses live results dashboard to identify gaps. 🟡 IEP: Extended to 60 seconds per question; definitions displayed alongside questions. |
| 15–35 min | **Constitutional Courtroom** | Mock trial activity. Present a fictional case: *"Springdale Middle School has banned all student cell phones on school property, including before and after school. A group of parents sues, arguing the school has exceeded its authority."* **Roles:** 2 student lawyers for the parents, 2 for the school, 3 Supreme Court justices, rest of class is jury/audience. Students use constitutional principles to make arguments: Does the school have this power? Is this "limited government"? What about the rule of law? 🔵 Advanced: Justices must write a 3-sentence "opinion" citing specific constitutional principles. |
| 35–45 min | **Summative Assessment Preview** | Distribute Day 5 assessment options: **Option A:** Traditional test (30 multiple choice + 2 short answer) **Option B:** Create a "Constitution in My Community" presentation (3-minute Slides presentation showing 3 examples of constitutional principles in action in Richmond, VA) Students choose their option and begin review/preparation. |
| 45–50 min | **Exit Ticket** | "Which constitutional principle do you think is the most important for protecting individual rights? Defend your answer in 2–3 sentences." |

**Materials:** Chromebooks (Quizizz code projected), mock trial case handout, role cards, assessment option descriptions

## Day 5: Unit Assessment (Friday)

**SOL Focus:** CE.2a–d, CE.6a–c — All unit standards
**Learning Objective:** SWBAT demonstrate mastery of constitutional structure, principles, and the system of checks and balances.

### Assessment Options

#### Option A: Written Test (50 min)

**Section 1: Multiple Choice (30 questions, 1 pt each)**
Sample questions:
1. The Preamble to the Constitution begins with the words "We the People" to emphasize which principle?
   a) Limited government  b) Consent of the governed  c) Rule of law  d) Separation of powers

2. Which branch of government has the power to declare laws unconstitutional?
   a) Legislative  b) Executive  c) Judicial  d) All three equally

3. If the President vetoes a bill, Congress can override the veto with —
   a) A simple majority  b) A two-thirds vote in both houses  c) A Supreme Court ruling  d) A new election

**Section 2: Short Answer (2 questions, 10 pts each)**

1. *Choose TWO principles of government (consent of the governed, limited government, rule of law, democracy, representative government). For each, define the principle and give a specific example of how it works in the United States today.* (CE.2c)

2. *The framers of the Constitution created a system of checks and balances. Explain how this system works by describing how each of the three branches can check the power of the other two. Use at least three specific examples.* (CE.6b-c)

**🟡 IEP Modifications:**
- Extended time (75 minutes instead of 50)
- Word bank provided for short-answer questions
- Multiple choice reduced to 20 questions (highest-value standards only)
- Short answer: choose 1 question instead of 2

#### Option B: "Constitution in My Community" Presentation

**Requirements:**
- 3-minute Google Slides presentation
- Identify 3 examples of constitutional principles in action in Richmond, VA
- For each example: name the principle, describe the real-world connection, explain why it matters
- Must include at least one image or visual per slide
- Presented to the class on Friday (or recorded if student prefers)

**Rubric (50 pts):**

| Category | Excellent (10) | Proficient (8) | Developing (6) | Beginning (4) |
|----------|---------------|----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Accuracy | All 3 principles correctly identified and explained | 2 of 3 correct | 1 of 3 correct | Principles not identified |
| Real-World Connection | Clear, specific local examples | General examples | Vague connections | No clear connection |
| Analysis Depth | Explains *why* each principle matters | Some analysis | Mostly descriptive | No analysis |
| Presentation Quality | Polished, confident, within time | Minor issues | Needs improvement | Unprepared |
| Visual Design | Professional, enhances content | Adequate | Minimal effort | No visuals |
