# Competitor Analysis Digest — Example Output

> This is what the agent team produces when you run this sample.

## Scenario

Elena Vasquez owns **Ember & Origin Coffee Roasters**, a specialty coffee roastery and café in Portland, Oregon's Alberta Arts District. She roasts single-origin beans in small batches and sells retail bags, wholesale to six local restaurants, and direct-to-consumer via her website. Her business does about $38K/month in revenue.

Elena wants to monitor three direct competitors weekly:

- **Stumptown Coffee Roasters** — the Portland heavyweight with national distribution
- **Coava Coffee Roasters** — a fellow specialty roaster on SE Grand Avenue known for single-origins
- **Good Coffee** — a growing multi-location Portland chain with a strong Instagram presence

She needs actionable intelligence she can review in 10–15 minutes every Monday morning.

## What the Agents Did

### Round 1: First Draft

**SpyBot** produced:

SpyBot compiled a 2,400-word briefing covering all three competitors. It pulled recent social media posts, website changes, and Google review snapshots. The report included a section for each competitor listing their latest Instagram posts, a summary of new menu items spotted on their websites, and a general "things to watch" paragraph. It noted that Stumptown had launched a new holiday blend, that Coava updated their website design, and that Good Coffee posted about a new location. The tone was informational and covered a wide surface area.

However, the report read more like a news clipping service than a strategic briefing. Pricing data was mentioned only once (Stumptown's new blend at $18.50/12oz) with no comparison to Elena's pricing. The "strategic implications" section was three generic sentences. Several items were noise — like Coava's website font change and a Stumptown repost of a customer photo — that had no business relevance. The recommendations were vague ("consider updating your holiday offerings").

**StrategyAnalyst** scored it **68/100** and found:

- **Signal-to-noise ratio is poor.** At least 30% of the content is irrelevant (website font changes, customer reposts). A busy owner will stop reading if the first items aren't actionable.
- **Missing price comparison tables.** The one price mentioned ($18.50 for Stumptown's holiday blend) isn't contextualized against Elena's pricing or Coava's. A roaster needs to see price-per-ounce across competitors at a glance.
- **No severity or priority ranking.** All items are presented with equal weight. A competitor opening a new location 8 blocks away is existential; a new Instagram filter is not.
- **Strategic analysis is generic.** "Consider updating your holiday offerings" could apply to any business. What specifically should Elena do, given her positioning as a small-batch single-origin roaster?
- **Missing review sentiment tracking.** No mention of what customers are saying about competitors on Google, Yelp, or Reddit. Review trends reveal competitor weaknesses Elena could exploit.
- **No hiring signal analysis.** Competitor job postings reveal strategic direction (e.g., hiring a wholesale manager = expansion plans). This was absent.
- **Recommendations lack specificity and timeline.** "Consider" is not an action. Give Elena specific moves with deadlines.

### Round 2: Improved Draft

**OpportunitySpotter** addressed the feedback:

The revised report was restructured around a priority-ranked format with a crisp executive summary up top. All noise items were removed. A price comparison table was added showing 12oz bag prices across all four roasters (including Elena's) with price-per-ounce calculations. Each competitor section now led with the highest-impact change and included a "So What?" callout explaining strategic relevance. A new review sentiment section tracked Google rating trends and highlighted recurring complaints. Hiring signals were added where found (Stumptown posting for a Pacific NW wholesale rep). Recommendations were converted to specific, time-bound actions rated by urgency. A trend synthesis section connected dots across competitors.

**StrategyAnalyst** scored it **93/100**: "Meets quality threshold."

## Trend Synthesis

Three cross-competitor trends emerged this week:

1. **Holiday season escalation is early and aggressive.** Stumptown launched a holiday blend at a discount price point. Good Coffee is pushing holiday merch. Coava released a premium limited-origin. Everyone is positioning for the gifting and holiday entertaining market 6 weeks out. You haven't announced your holiday plans yet — you're behind.

2. **The "experience economy" is intensifying.** Good Coffee's Alberta build-out emphasizes communal space, Instagram aesthetics, and food. Coava's blog post focuses on origin storytelling. Customers are buying experiences, not just coffee. Your 1,400 sq ft café with 28 seats is cozy but may feel cramped when a 2,100 sq ft competitor opens down the block.

3. **Wholesale is in flux.** Coava's price increase and Stumptown's new wholesale hire both signal movement in the B2B segment. The wholesale landscape is reshuffling, and your competitive pricing ($11.50/lb) is suddenly a significant advantage.

## Recommended Actions

### 🔴 Immediate (This Week — by November 22)

1. **Prepare an Alberta Street loyalty counter-offensive.** Before Good Coffee opens on Dec 7, launch a "Neighborhood Original" campaign. Specific steps: (a) Design a loyalty card offering every 8th drink free (current: none), (b) create an "Alberta Regulars" punch card with a holiday bonus (buy 5 bags in December, get a free limited-edition mug), (c) budget: ~$400 for card printing and 50 mugs. **Owner action: Approve budget and brief your designer by Friday.**

2. **Contact Navarre and Tusk about wholesale.** Both are publicly unhappy about Coava's price increase. Your $11.50/lb rate is now $0.75 cheaper. Prepare a sample kit (3 blends, your wholesale rate card, your origin story one-pager). **Owner action: Email both restaurant managers by Wednesday offering a free tasting session.**

3. **Announce your holiday offering.** You're the only competitor without a holiday product in market. Options: (a) Release your Ethiopian Yirgacheffe in holiday packaging at the same $20.50 but with a free tasting card, or (b) create a $35 gift set (12oz bag + branded mug + tasting notes). **Owner action: Decide on holiday SKU by Thursday and brief your roaster.**

### 🟡 Short-Term (Next 2 Weeks — by December 6)

4. **Amplify your quality advantage in reviews.** Your 4.6 Google rating is your moat. Before Good Coffee opens, email your top 20 regulars asking for Google reviews. Target: 15 new reviews before Dec 7 to build a buffer. Include a small thank-you (free drip coffee on next visit).

5. **Scout the Good Coffee Alberta build-out.** Walk by 2205 NE Alberta this week and next. Note: seating layout, menu board (if visible), hours posted, hiring signs. Report back for next week's digest. **Owner action: Do a walk-by twice before Dec 7.**

6. **Counter Stumptown's subscription promo.** Don't discount — instead, add value. Offer a "Roaster's Letter" with each subscription shipment in December: a handwritten-style note about the origin, your roasting decisions, and a brewing tip. Cost: nearly zero. Differentiation: high. Stumptown can't do personal at scale.

### 🟢 Longer-Term (December–January)

7. **Develop a "Good Coffee opened — here's our edge" playbook.** After their first 2 weeks, we'll have data on their traffic, menu, pricing, and customer reception. Plan a January strategy session to review and adapt. For now, focus on locking in your regulars and your quality narrative.

8. **Explore a collaborative Alberta Street promotion** with non-competing neighbors (the bookstore, the boutique, the gallery). A "Shop Alberta, Support Local" holiday initiative could drive foot traffic to your end of the block while Good Coffee draws traffic to theirs. Rising tide strategy.

9. **Evaluate TikTok.** Good Coffee is building a presence (12K views this week) and you have none. Short-form video of roasting, latte art, and origin stories performs well in coffee. Low-cost experiment: post 2 videos/week for 4 weeks in January and measure. Budget: your time only.
