## Scenario

Priya is a senior software engineer with 7 years of experience. She's been interviewing at TechCorp for a "Senior SWE II" role. After 5 rounds of interviews, they've extended a verbal offer: $145K base, 15K signing bonus, standard benefits. Priya knows from Levels.fyi that the market rate for this role at TechCorp is $160-180K, and she has a competing offer from another company at $158K. She wants to negotiate but has never negotiated a tech offer before and is worried about seeming "ungrateful" or having the offer rescinded.

**Negotiator** produced:

- Mentions competing offers too early and vaguely ("other offers")—sounds like bluffing

**Timeline:**  
- They expect response in 1 week (standard)  
- Your competing offer expires in 10 days (you have breathing room)

**Your Mindset:**  
You're not being greedy. You're having a business conversation about compensation that reflects your value. TechCorp EXPECTS negotiation (80% of tech candidates negotiate). Not negotiating might actually signal lack of confidence.

## Opening Script

**Context**: You'll likely have this conversation via phone/Zoom with the recruiter or hiring manager. They'll say something like, "Have you had a chance to review the offer?"

**Your Response (memorize the key beats, not word-for-word):**

> "Yes! Thank you so much for the offer—I've been really thinking about it, and I'm genuinely excited. The conversations I had with [Hiring Manager Name] about [specific project or team challenge you discussed] really got me energized, and I can see myself making a big impact on [specific team goal].
>
> I do want to discuss the compensation package, and I'm hoping we can work together to get to a place where it reflects both the market and the value I'd bring to the team.
>
> I've done some research using Levels.fyi, Blind, and conversations with folks in similar roles, and I'm seeing Senior SWE II roles at TechCorp typically ranging from $160K to $180K in base salary, with total comp in the $220-260K range when you include equity and bonuses.
>
> The offer you extended—$145K base and $15K signing—is about 10-15% below that range. Given my 7 years of experience, my background in [your specialty—e.g., 'distributed systems and ML infrastructure'], and the complexity of the problems we discussed, I was hoping we could get the base salary to **$165,000**.
>
> I know that's a $20K increase, and I want to be clear: I'm not trying to be difficult. This is about making sure the offer reflects market rate and aligns with what I'd be contributing. Does that feel like something we can work toward?"

**Then: STOP TALKING.** Let them respond. Count to 5 in your head if you need to. Silence is okay. Don't fill it with "I mean, if not, that's okay" or backpedaling.

## Common Objections & Your Responses

### Objection 1: "That's above our budget for this level."

**Your Response:**

> "I understand budget constraints are real. Can I ask—is the concern about the level, or about the specific number? Because I'm seeing $165K as the median for Senior SWE II on Levels.fyi, and I'd be coming in with 7 years of experience and [specific in-demand skill].
>
> If the concern is about level, would it make sense to discuss bumping this to Senior SWE III or Staff Engineer, given my background? Or if the base salary is fixed, could we explore increasing the equity or signing bonus to get closer to that $165K total value in year one?"

**Why this works:** You're showing flexibility (multiple paths), providing data (not just feelings), and positioning it as a collaborative problem-solve.

---

### Objection 2: "We don't typically go that high for new hires."

**Your Response:**

> "I appreciate that, and I definitely want to respect your internal equity. That said, I'm also looking at this from a market competitiveness lens. I have a competing offer at $158K base from [DataCo], which suggests the market rate for someone with my background is in that range.
>
> I'm more excited about TechCorp because of [specific reason—team, mission, growth opportunity], but I need the offer to be competitive. If $165K isn't possible, could we meet in the middle at $160K? That would make the decision a lot easier for me."

**Why this works:** You've now revealed the competing offer (with specifics), you've shown preference for TechCorp (flattering but honest), and you've given them an easier "yes" option ($160K).

---

### Objection 3: "We can't move on base salary, but we can offer more equity."

**Your Response:**

> "I really appreciate the flexibility. Equity is definitely valuable, especially at a company with TechCorp's growth trajectory. Can you walk me through what equity looks like for this role?
>
> Specifically: What's the total dollar value, over how many years does it vest, and what's the strike price or valuation it's based on?
>
> If we can't get to $165K in base, I'd want the equity package to close that $20K annual gap—so roughly $80K in additional equity over 4 years, which is about $20K per year. Does that feel doable?"

**Why this works:** You're not rejecting their counteroffer, you're engaging with it and doing the math transparently. You're also showing you understand equity (not just taking their word that it's "generous").

**Follow-up question if they agree:** "One more thing—could we front-load the equity vesting a bit? Instead of a standard 4-year vest, could we do a 3-year vest or a larger year-1 grant? That would help offset the lower base salary in my first year."

---

### Objection 4: "We're already offering above market for this role."

**Your Response (stay calm, don't get defensive):**

> "I appreciate that perspective, and I definitely don't want to waste either of our time if we're not aligned. I'm curious—what data are you using for market rate? I'm seeing [cite your sources: Levels.fyi median of $168K, Blind reports of $160-175K, your friend's offer at $163K].
>
> It's possible we're looking at different data sets, or maybe there's something about the role that I'm misunderstanding—like it's scoped differently than a typical Senior SWE II? I'd love to understand what you're seeing."

**Why this works:** You're not saying they're wrong, you're asking for their data. This either (a) reveals they're using outdated data, or (b) clarifies that the role is actually more junior than the title suggests (in which case, you can decide if you want to proceed).

---

### Objection 5: "If you're just going to take the DataCo offer, we should stop now."

**Your Response (this is rare, but if they get snippy):**

> "I really appreciate your directness, and I want to be direct too: I'm not trying to play games or create a bidding war. I'm here because I'm genuinely excited about TechCorp—[specific reason].
>
> The DataCo offer is a data point that validates what I'm seeing in the market, but it's not the only thing driving my ask. I'm seeing this salary range across multiple data sources, and I'm confident it reflects the value of this role.
>
> I'm hoping we can find a path forward that works for both of us. If $165K isn't possible, are there other components—like PTO, remote flexibility, or a title adjustment—that we could discuss?"

**Why this works:** You've reasserted your interest (sincerely), you've re-anchored on data (not just the competing offer), and you've opened the door to alternatives. If they're still hostile after this, that's a 🚩 about the company culture.

## Non-Monetary Alternatives

If they truly can't move on base salary or equity, here's what else you can negotiate:

### 1. **Signing Bonus** ($15K → $25-30K)
- **Pitch**: "If base salary is fixed, could we increase the signing bonus from $15K to $30K? That would help offset the gap in year one and give me a stronger start."
- **Why companies like this**: One-time cost, doesn't affect salary bands long-term

### 2. **Equity Refresh** (Annual top-ups)
- **Pitch**: "Could we build in an annual equity refresh so my total comp stays competitive over time? I've seen TechCorp does this for high performers."
- **Why companies like this**: Ties to performance, retains employees

### 3. **PTO** (Standard → +5 days)
- **Pitch**: "If we can't move on comp, could we add 5 extra PTO days? I value work-life balance, and that would be meaningful."
- **Cost to company**: ~$2,800 (for $145K salary) but feels generous

### 4. **Remote Flexibility** (3 days in-office → 2 days or fully remote)
- **Pitch**: "Would there be flexibility to work remotely 4 days per week instead of the standard 3? Or fully remote? That would significantly improve my quality of life."
- **Why companies might say yes**: Post-COVID, many tech companies are flexible on this

### 5. **Title Bump** (Senior SWE II → Senior SWE III or Staff Engineer)
- **Pitch**: "Given my 7 years of experience and the scope we discussed, would it make sense to adjust the level to Senior SWE III or Staff? That might also resolve the compensation discussion."
- **Why companies might say yes**: Titles are free, and it might unlock a higher salary band

### 6. **Professional Development Budget** ($2-5K/year)
- **Pitch**: "Could TechCorp provide a $5K annual learning budget for conferences, courses, or certifications? I'm committed to staying on the cutting edge, and I know that benefits the team."
- **Why companies like this**: Shows you're growth-oriented

### 7. **Earlier Performance Review** (12 months → 6 months)
- **Pitch**: "If we start at $145K, could we schedule a performance and comp review at 6 months instead of 12? That way, if I'm delivering the value we discussed, we can adjust sooner."
- **Why companies might say yes**: Doesn't commit them now, but shows your confidence

## Silence & Pivot Points

**When to PAUSE and wait for them to speak:**

1. ✅ **After making your initial ask** ($165K pitch) — Don't fill the silence. Let them process.
2. ✅ **After they make a counter-offer** — Don't immediately accept or reject. Pause. Say, "Let me think about that for a second."
3. ✅ **After asking a question** ("What data are you using for market rate?") — Let them answer fully.

**When to PIVOT to alternatives:**

1. ⚠️ **If they say no to $165K twice** — Move to $160K or non-monetary asks
2. ⚠️ **If they seem frustrated or annoyed** — Acknowledge their position: "I hear you, and I really appreciate you working with me on this. Let me think about what else might work."
3. ⚠️ **If they offer something close to your target** ($160K instead of $165K) — Don't keep pushing. Say, "That's really close to what I was hoping for. Can I think about it overnight and get back to you tomorrow?" (Gives you space to decide if you're happy.)

**When to ASK QUESTIONS instead of making statements:**

- "Can you help me understand how TechCorp thinks about equity for this role?"  
- "What would need to be true for us to get to $165K?"  
- "Is there flexibility in how the comp is structured, even if the total budget is fixed?"  

Questions keep the conversation collaborative. Statements can feel like ultimatums.

## Walk-Away Script

**If they can't meet your minimum ($158K total comp equivalent) and you decide DataCo is better:**

> "I really appreciate your time and the offer. After thinking it through, I don't think I can make the numbers work given my other opportunity. TechCorp is a fantastic company, and I loved everyone I talked to. I hope we can stay in touch, and maybe there's an opportunity to work together in the future when the timing or level is a better fit. Thank you again."

**Why this is good:**
- Polite and professional (you might interview here again in 2 years!)  
- Leaves the door open  
- Doesn't burn bridges or make them feel bad  
- Firm but kind  

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ "Your offer is insulting"  
- ❌ "I can't believe you won't negotiate"  
- ❌ "DataCo is way better anyway"

## Final Negotiation Checklist

Before you get on the call:

- [ ] Review your target number ($165K) and walk-away number ($158K equivalent)
- [ ] Have your data open (Levels.fyi, Blind, competing offer details)  
- [ ] Practice saying "$165K" out loud 5 times (so it doesn't feel weird in the moment)  
- [ ] Write down 3 specific reasons you're excited about TechCorp (so you sound genuine)  
- [ ] Prepare to PAUSE after asking (don't fill silence)  
- [ ] Have a glass of water nearby (dry mouth is real in negotiations!)  
- [ ] Remind yourself: **You're worth it. This is a business conversation. They expect this.**  

**After the call:**
- [ ] Send a follow-up email summarizing what you discussed (keeps everyone aligned)
- [ ] If they make a new offer, take 24 hours to think (don't accept on the spot unless it's your ideal number)
- [ ] Run the new offer by a friend or mentor (fresh eyes help)  
- [ ] Trust your gut (if the negotiation felt adversarial, that's a culture signal)
