## Understanding Biscuit's Behavior

**What's happening:** Biscuit isn't being "bad" or "disobedient." Separation anxiety is a panic response — his nervous system is telling him that being alone is dangerous. This is especially common in:
- Shelter dogs (Biscuit experienced abandonment/rehoming)
- Puppies under 6 months (still developing emotional regulation)
- Dogs who bond strongly with one person (Biscuit with Amy)

**What this means for training:** We can't punish the anxiety out of him (punishment increases anxiety). We also can't "tough love" it — leaving him to cry it out makes it worse. Instead, we'll systematically teach his brain that being alone is safe, boring, and even rewarding.

**The core principle:** Desensitization — we expose Biscuit to being alone in doses small enough that he doesn't panic, then gradually increase the duration. Every successful repetition teaches his brain: "She left, and she came back. Nothing bad happened."

---

## Before You Start: Baseline Assessment

Before Week 1, spend one day assessing Biscuit's exact threshold. This tells you where to start training.

**How to test:**
1. Set up a phone camera or laptop webcam (Zoom/FaceTime yourself, or use the free "Dog Monitor" app)
2. Give Biscuit a stuffed Kong
3. Leave the room and close the door (stay right outside)
4. Watch the video feed and note the EXACT moment Biscuit stops eating and starts showing stress signs

**Stress signs to watch for:**
- Stops eating the Kong and looks at the door
- Pacing
- Whining or barking
- Panting (when it's not hot)
- Drooling
- Scratching at the door

**Record your findings:**

| Duration Alone | Biscuit's Behavior |
|---------------|-------------------|
| 0–2 min | Eating Kong, relaxed |
| 2–5 min | Finishes Kong, looks at door, mild whining |
| 5–8 min | Pacing, barking starts |
| 8+ min | Continuous barking, scratching at door |

**Biscuit's estimated threshold: ~5 minutes.** This means we start training at **3 minutes** (below threshold, so he succeeds every time).

## The 6-Week Plan

### Week 1: Departure Cue Desensitization (January 20–26)

**Goal:** Break the association between departure cues (keys, shoes, coat) and Amy leaving.

Right now, Biscuit starts panicking before Amy even leaves because he's learned that certain cues predict departure: picking up keys, putting on shoes, grabbing a coat, picking up a purse. We need to "bore out" these triggers.

**Daily Exercises (3× per day, 10 minutes each):**

**Exercise 1: Keys Desensitization**
1. Pick up your keys. Walk around the house. Put them down. (No reaction from Biscuit → treat)
2. Repeat 5 times.
3. Pick up keys, walk to the front door. Touch the doorknob. Walk away. Put keys down. (Treat)
4. Repeat 5 times.
5. Pick up keys, open the door, close it without going through. Put keys down. (Treat)
6. Repeat 5 times.

**Exercise 2: Shoes & Coat**
1. Put on your shoes. Sit on the couch for 10 minutes. Take them off.
2. Put on your coat. Make a sandwich. Take it off.
3. Put on shoes AND coat AND pick up keys. Feed Biscuit dinner. Take everything off.
4. The goal: Biscuit sees these cues and thinks "who knows what's happening" instead of "SHE'S LEAVING."

**Exercise 3: Door Practice**
1. Walk to the front door. Open it. Close it. Walk back to the couch. (Treat)
2. Walk to the door. Step outside. Immediately step back in. (Treat — huge party!)
3. Walk to the door. Step outside. Close the door. Count to 5. Come back in. (Treat)
4. Walk to the door. Step outside. Close the door. Count to 10. Come back in. (Treat)

**Milestone for Week 1:** Biscuit shows no visible stress when you pick up keys, put on shoes, or touch the front door. He may wag his tail (anticipating treats) instead of pacing.

---

### Week 2: Short Absences — Building to 10 Minutes (January 27 – February 2)

**Goal:** Biscuit stays calm for up to 10 minutes alone.

**The Protocol (3–5 sessions per day, 5 minutes each):**

| Day | Session 1 | Session 2 | Session 3 | Session 4 |
|-----|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Mon | 3 min | 2 min | 4 min | 3 min |
| Tue | 4 min | 3 min | 5 min | 4 min |
| Wed | 5 min | 3 min | 6 min | 5 min |
| Thu | 6 min | 4 min | 7 min | 5 min |
| Fri | 7 min | 5 min | 8 min | 6 min |
| Sat | 8 min | 5 min | 9 min | 7 min |
| Sun | 10 min | 6 min | 8 min | 10 min |

**Important pattern:** Notice how the durations go UP and DOWN, not just up. This is intentional. A straight increase (3, 4, 5, 6, 7...) teaches the dog that each absence is longer than the last, which increases anxiety. Mixing shorter sessions between longer ones teaches that "sometimes she leaves for a long time, sometimes a short time — it's unpredictable and all fine."

**Before each session:**
1. 20-minute walk or play session (burn energy)
2. Give Biscuit a frozen stuffed Kong (recipe below)
3. No big goodbye — just calmly walk out
4. Monitor via camera

**After each session:**
1. Walk in calmly — no excited greeting for 30 seconds
2. If Biscuit was calm: quiet praise + treat
3. If Biscuit was showing stress when you returned: session was too long. Next session, drop back by 2 minutes.

**The Golden Rule:** End every session BEFORE Biscuit gets distressed. If he was calm at 6 minutes yesterday but panics at 7 minutes today, that's okay — go back to 5 minutes tomorrow. Progress is not linear.

**Milestone for Week 2:** Biscuit is reliably calm for 10 minutes alone with no whining, barking, pacing, or destructive behavior.

### Week 3: Medium Absences — Building to 30 Minutes (February 3–9)

**Goal:** Biscuit stays calm for up to 30 minutes alone.

| Day | Session 1 | Session 2 | Session 3 |
|-----|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Mon | 10 min | 8 min | 12 min |
| Tue | 15 min | 10 min | 12 min |

| Thu | 20 min | 12 min | 15 min |
| Fri | 15 min | 22 min | 18 min |
| Sat | 25 min | 15 min | 20 min |

**New this week — "real" departures:**
- Actually get in the car and drive around the block
- Go to the mailbox, walk to a neighbor's house
- The dog needs to hear the car leave AND return without incident

**Enrichment upgrade:** In addition to the frozen Kong, add a snuffle mat or scatter-feed Biscuit's breakfast kibble around the room before leaving. This gives him 10–15 minutes of foraging activity.

**Milestone for Week 3:** Biscuit is reliably calm for 30 minutes. You should see him settle into a resting position (lying down) within 5 minutes of your departure on the camera.

## When to Call a Professional

Contact a **veterinary behaviorist** (not just a regular vet or a "dog trainer") if:

- [ ] Biscuit shows no improvement after 2 weeks of consistent training
- [ ] He injures himself trying to escape (broken nails, bloody paws, broken teeth)
- [ ] He refuses high-value food (frozen Kong) even when you're present — this suggests anxiety beyond what behavioral training alone can address
- [ ] He has panic-level responses (drooling excessively, urinating/defecating, self-harm) at ANY duration, even 1 minute

**Medication is not a failure.** For severe separation anxiety, a veterinary behaviorist may recommend fluoxetine (Reconcile) or other anti-anxiety medication to lower Biscuit's baseline anxiety while training takes effect. Medication + training is more effective than either alone for moderate-to-severe cases.

## Weekend Trip Preparation (March 1–2)

By Week 6, Biscuit should be comfortable alone for 4 hours. For an overnight trip:

**Option A: Pet sitter stays at your home** (Best option — Biscuit stays in his familiar environment)
- Cost: $50–75/night via Rover or local referral
- Leave detailed instructions: feeding schedule, walk times, frozen Kong routine, emergency vet info
- Do a trial overnight: have the sitter come for dinner the week before so Biscuit meets them

**Option B: Biscuit stays at sitter's home**
- Cost: $40–60/night via Rover
- Bring Biscuit's bed, a worn t-shirt of Amy's (scent comfort), frozen Kongs, and his usual food
- Do a trial day visit first

**Option C: Boarding facility**
- Cost: $45–65/night
- Last resort for a dog with separation anxiety — boarding can be stressful
- If you board, choose a facility with individual rooms (not kennel runs) and ask about their anxiety protocol

## Progress Expectations

| Week | Expected Behavior | Red Flag |
|------|-------------------|----------|
| 1 | No reaction to departure cues (keys, shoes, coat) | Still panicking at cues → more repetitions needed |
| 2 | Calm for 10 min alone; eating Kong throughout | Can't reach 5 min without barking → consult professional |
| 3 | Calm for 30 min; settles into resting position | Destroying items during practice → anxiety too high, drop back |
| 4 | Calm for 60 min; sleeps during absence | Regression after progress → normal, don't panic, add extra short sessions |
| 5 | Calm for 2–3 hours; handles real departures | Neighbor reports barking during "real" absences → duration too high for real-life, use management plan longer |
| 6 | Calm for 4+ hours; ready for pet sitter | Panics with sitter → sitter needs multiple intro visits |

**Remember:** Progress is not linear. You will have bad days. Biscuit might nail 30 minutes on Thursday and panic at 15 minutes on Friday. That's normal. When in doubt, make the next session shorter and easier. Success builds confidence — for both of you.
