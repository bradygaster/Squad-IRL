# Parent Email Drafter — Example Output

> This is what the agent team produces when you run this sample.

## Scenario

Mr. Chen is a 4th grade teacher. One of his students, Maya, has been struggling with homework completion for the past 3 weeks—turning in only 4 out of 15 assignments. Maya is bright and engaged in class, but something's clearly going on at home. Mr. Chen needs to email Maya's parents (both work full-time) to address the issue without sounding accusatory or making them defensive. He wants their partnership, not their panic.

## What the Agents Did

### Round 1: First Draft

**Communicator** produced:

**Subject:** Concern About Maya's Homework

Dear Mr. and Mrs. Patterson,

I wanted to reach out regarding Maya's homework completion. Over the past three weeks, she has turned in only 4 out of 15 assignments. This is affecting her grades and I'm concerned about her falling behind. Homework is an important part of reinforcing classroom learning and developing responsibility.

Please let me know if there's anything going on that I should be aware of. We need to work together to get Maya back on track with her assignments. Let me know if you'd like to set up a meeting to discuss this.

Mr. Chen

**ToneManager** scored it **71/100** and found:

- Leads with the problem, not positives—parents immediately feel defensive
- "Concern" in subject line triggers parental anxiety before they even read it
- Language feels like lecture ("homework is important," "need to work together")
- Doesn't acknowledge Maya's strengths or what IS going well
- No specific examples of Maya's in-class behavior (context missing)
- Vague action items—what exactly are you asking parents to do?

### Round 2: Improved Draft

**SolutionFocuser** addressed the feedback:

Changed subject line to neutral/positive framing, opened with specific strengths about Maya, presented homework issue as collaborative puzzle to solve together rather than a problem to fix, provided concrete examples of Maya's engagement in class, suggested low-pressure ways parents can investigate at home, and included specific next steps with flexible options for communication.

**ToneManager** scored it **92/100**: "Meets quality threshold. Partnership-focused, empathetic, and actionable."

## Final Output

**Subject:** Checking In - Maya's Been a Star in Class Discussions!

Dear Mr. and Mrs. Patterson,

I hope this email finds you well! I wanted to share some great moments from class and touch base about something I'm noticing with Maya.

First, the highlights: Maya has been absolutely stellar in our science unit on ecosystems. Last week, she made a connection between our classroom terrarium and her grandmother's garden that completely shifted our discussion—even I hadn't thought of it that way! She's clearly a visual learner who loves hands-on activities, and her contributions in group work have been excellent. Her classmates often look to her for ideas.

I'm reaching out because I've noticed a pattern over the past three weeks that I wanted to loop you in on. Maya's been turning in homework assignments less frequently than usual—about 4 out of the last 15. What's interesting is that this is only happening with take-home work; her in-class work remains strong, and she's fully engaged during lessons.

Because Maya is so capable and enthusiastic in class, I'm wondering if something has shifted at home that's making homework harder to complete. Sometimes it's a practical issue (lost planner, chaotic after-school schedule), sometimes it's about the work itself (instructions unclear when she gets home, or she's not sure where to start), and sometimes life just gets busy and overwhelming for families.

**Here's how I'm thinking we can partner on this:**

**What I'm doing:**
- I've started a brief check-in with Maya at dismissal to make sure she knows what the homework is and has the materials
- I'm going to have her show me her assignment notebook before she packs up (just to make sure nothing's getting lost in translation)
- I'll continue to give her positive reinforcement for in-class work while we sort this out

**What might help at home (totally optional - you know your family best):**
- A quick, low-pressure chat with Maya about how homework is going—sometimes kids are dealing with something (distractions, sibling interruptions, not understanding the assignment) but don't know how to bring it up
- If there's a scheduling challenge (activities, bedtime battles, etc.), I'm happy to work with you on timing or quantity of homework
- If she's missing assignments because she's not writing them down, we can problem-solve that together

I'm also wondering: Have you noticed anything different with Maya lately? Any changes in her mood, sleep, or general routine? Sometimes kids compartmentalize and I only see the "school Maya" while home is where other stuff surfaces.

**Next Steps - You Choose:**
- **If you'd like to talk by phone:** I'm available Tuesdays and Thursdays after 3:30pm. Just reply with a time that works.
- **If you'd prefer to start with email:** Just let me know what you're seeing at home and we can problem-solve together in writing.
- **If you want to try some strategies first and then check back in:** Totally fine! Give it a week or two and let me know how it's going.

No pressure or urgency here—Maya is not "in trouble" and her grades are still solid. I'm just catching this early because I care about her success and I know that when we work together, we can usually figure out what a kid needs.

Thank you for being such responsive and involved parents. Maya is lucky to have you, and I'm lucky to have her in my class. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you have any questions or if there's anything I should know.

## Alternative Version: If the Issue is More Urgent

*(The agent team also produced this variation for when the homework problem is more severe or time-sensitive)*

**Subject:** Partnership Needed - Let's Problem-Solve Together About Maya

Dear Mr. and Mrs. Patterson,

I hope you're doing well. I'm reaching out because I need your partnership on something happening with Maya, and I think together we can figure it out.

**What I'm seeing in class:**  
Maya is bright, engaged, and participates actively. She's doing well on in-class assignments and clearly understands the material. Her peers enjoy working with her, and she brings great energy to discussions.

**What I'm seeing with homework:**  
Over the past three weeks, Maya has turned in only 4 out of 15 homework assignments. This is a significant change from the first two months of school when she was completing most assignments. I've checked in with her a couple of times, and she's given short answers ("I forgot," "I couldn't find it") but hasn't opened up about what's really going on.

**Why I'm reaching out now:**  
When a capable student suddenly struggles with homework but not class work, it usually means something's getting in the way—and it's rarely about the kid not caring. I've seen this with many students, and it's almost always something solvable once we understand what it is.

Some common culprits I've seen:
- Schedule overwhelm (too many activities, not enough downtime)
- Missing organizational piece (planner not working, backpack is a black hole)
- Sibling or home distractions during homework time  
- Anxiety about getting it "perfect" so avoidance kicks in  
- Material is actually confusing but Maya doesn't want to admit it  
- Something else going on (friendship issue, worries, change in routine)

**What I'm asking:**  
Could we set up a 15-20 minute phone call this week? I'd love to hear what you're seeing at home and share some strategies that have worked for other families. Together we can figure out what Maya needs.

I have availability:
- Tuesday, March 19: 3:30-5:00pm  
- Wednesday, March 20: 7:30-8:00am (before school) or 3:30-5:00pm  
- Thursday, March 21: 3:30-5:00pm

If none of those work, I can also do a quick Zoom/phone call during my lunch (12:15-12:35pm any day this week).

Maya is a wonderful student and person. Let's figure this out together so she can feel successful both in class and at home.

Looking forward to connecting,  
Mr. Chen
