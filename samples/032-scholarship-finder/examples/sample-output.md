## 5. Draft Essay: EPA GRO Fellowship Personal Statement

**Prompt:** "Describe your interest in environmental science and how your experiences have prepared you for a career in this field." (1,000 words)

### Draft Opening (first 300 words — to be expanded)

---

The first time I pulled garlic mustard from the forest floor at Springbrook Prairie Forest Preserve, I didn't understand why it mattered. It was a Saturday morning in October of my sophomore year, the kind of gray Illinois day that makes you question every decision that got you out of bed. My Environmental Action Club had partnered with the DuPage County Forest Preserve District for a restoration workday, and I'd signed up mostly because it counted toward NHS volunteer hours.

But then Ranger Michelle knelt beside me and showed me what I was actually doing. "See this?" she said, pulling back a mat of garlic mustard to reveal a tiny cluster of trillium shoots underneath. "These have been waiting three years for enough light to grow. You just gave them that."

That was the moment environmental science became personal. Not the textbook definition of invasive species management — I'd memorized that for AP Bio — but the visceral understanding that ecosystems are interconnected systems where one intervention cascades into dozens of outcomes. Over the next two years, I returned to Springbrook Prairie 47 times. I logged over 200 volunteer hours. I watched those trillium shoots become flowers.

But I also started asking harder questions. Why was garlic mustard spreading faster in the southeast quadrant? Was it correlated with the new stormwater runoff from the housing development on 83rd Street? I designed a simple study — quadrat sampling across four zones, measuring garlic mustard density against distance from the runoff channel — and presented my findings to the Forest Preserve ecology team. They're now monitoring the area I flagged.

This experience taught me something no classroom could: environmental science isn't just about understanding ecosystems. It's about intervening in them responsibly, with data, humility, and a willingness to get your hands dirty — literally.

## 6. Draft Essay: USTA Foundation Scholarship

**Prompt:** "How has tennis shaped your character?" (500 words)

### Draft

---

I am not the best tennis player on my team. I'm second singles — not first — and I've never won a sectional title. But tennis taught me something that winning never could: how to compete with yourself.

In tennis, there's no coach calling plays from the sideline. No teammates to pick up your slack. When you double-fault at 4-5 in the third set, the only person who can fix your toss is you. I learned this the hard way during my sophomore season when I lost seven consecutive third-set tiebreakers. Seven. My coach, Mr. Yamamoto, didn't give me a motivational speech. He gave me a journal and told me to write down what I was thinking at 5-5 in every tiebreaker.

The pattern was embarrassingly clear. At 5-5, I stopped thinking about hitting the ball and started thinking about losing. My first serves got cautious. My groundstrokes drifted to the center of the court. I was playing not to lose instead of playing to win.

Mr. Yamamoto's solution was counterintuitive: "At 5-5, I want you to hit the most aggressive shot you've got. If you miss, fine. But you're going to train your brain to associate pressure with action, not fear."

I lost the next two tiebreakers swinging for the fences. But by the third one, something clicked. At 5-5, I hit a forehand inside-out winner that I'd never have attempted two months earlier. I won that tiebreaker 7-5. And I won the next four.

This mindset — associating pressure with action — has shaped how I approach everything else. When my Environmental Action Club's recycling proposal got rejected by the school board, I didn't retreat. I asked for their specific objections, revised the proposal, and presented again. It passed unanimously. When my AP Chemistry grade dropped to a B+ after the first exam, I didn't just study harder — I changed my study method entirely, switching from re-reading notes to teaching concepts to my study group.

Tennis taught me that the moment you feel the most pressure is the moment that defines you. Not because winning under pressure proves you're talented, but because choosing to act — to swing hard, to show up again, to revise and resubmit — proves you're resilient.

I'm still second singles. And I've made peace with that, because the ranking doesn't measure what tennis actually gave me: the ability to look at a high-pressure moment and think, *This is where I hit my best shot.*
