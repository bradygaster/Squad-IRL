# Book Club Discussion Guide — Example Output

> This is what the agent team produces when you run this sample.

## Scenario

Jennifer Walsh leads a 12-person book club that meets monthly at the Elm Street Library in Brookline, Massachusetts. This month they're discussing *The Midnight Library* by Matt Haig. The group is mixed — ages 28 to 67, some voracious readers and some who read casually. Their last few meetings have felt flat, with discussion dying after 20 minutes. Jennifer wants a guide with 20 thought-provoking questions organized by theme, plus icebreakers and activities to keep the conversation alive for a full 90-minute session.

## What the Agents Did

### Round 1: First Draft

**Reader** produced:

```
THE MIDNIGHT LIBRARY - DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
1. Did you like the book?
2. What was your favorite part?
3. Did you like Nora?
4. What would you do in the Midnight Library?
5. Would you recommend this book?
```

**ThemeSpotter** scored it **49/100** and found:

- Only 5 questions instead of the requested 20
- Questions are surface-level yes/no prompts that kill conversation ("Did you like the book?" → "Yes." → silence)
- No thematic organization — questions are random rather than building on each other
- Missing any questions about the book's core themes (regret, depression, parallel lives, what makes a good life)
- No discussion of specific scenes, characters, or plot points
- No structure for a 90-minute meeting (icebreakers, pacing, activities)
- Question 4 is the only interesting one but it's framed as hypothetical rather than connecting to the text

### Round 2: Improved Draft

**ConversationStarter** addressed the feedback:

- Expanded to 20 questions organized across 5 thematic categories
- Replaced yes/no questions with open-ended prompts that invite storytelling and debate
- Added specific page/chapter references so members can find relevant passages
- Created a 90-minute meeting structure with warm-up, deep dive, and closing
- Included 2 icebreaker activities and 1 closing activity
- Added "conversation rescue" prompts for when discussion stalls
- Balanced questions between text analysis and personal connection

**ThemeSpotter** scored it **95/100**: "Rich, layered questions that will sustain 90+ minutes easily. The thematic organization lets the facilitator adapt on the fly."

# 📚 Book Club Discussion Guide: *The Midnight Library*

**Author:** Matt Haig
**Published:** 2020
**Pages:** 288
**Genre:** Literary fiction / Speculative fiction
**Group:** Elm Street Library Book Club (12 members)
**Meeting Date:** Third Thursday of the month, 7:00–8:30 PM
**Facilitator:** Jennifer Walsh

---

## Meeting Structure (90 Minutes)

| Time | Section | Purpose |
|------|---------|---------|
| 7:00–7:10 | Icebreaker Activity | Warm up, get everyone talking |
| 7:10–7:15 | Quick Reactions Round | First impressions, rating |
| 7:15–7:45 | Deep Dive Part 1 — Regret & Choice | Core themes |
| 7:45–8:00 | Deep Dive Part 2 — Identity & Depression | Heavier themes (handle with care) |
| 8:00–8:15 | Deep Dive Part 3 — The Philosophy of a Good Life | Big questions |
| 8:15–8:25 | Lightning Round — Fun & Debatable | Lighter energy, keep it lively |
| 8:25–8:30 | Closing Activity + Next Month's Pick | Wrap up warmly |

---

## Icebreaker (7:00–7:10)

### Activity: "The Book of Regrets"

*This directly connects to the book's central premise.*

**Instructions:** Before the meeting, place a small slip of paper and a pen at each person's seat. As people arrive, ask them to write down **one small regret** — nothing heavy, keep it light. Examples: "I regret not learning to play guitar," "I regret selling my '98 Camry," "I regret choosing accounting over art school."

Fold the slips and put them in a bowl. Jennifer reads them aloud one at a time. The group guesses who wrote each one. After the reveal, ask: "If you could live the version of your life where you didn't have that regret, would you?"

**Why this works:** It mirrors Nora's experience in the Midnight Library, gets people talking about themselves (which book clubs often skip), and creates a natural bridge into the first discussion question.

**Time:** ~8 minutes (allow the laughter and tangents — that's the point)

---

## Quick Reactions Round (7:10–7:15)

Go around the room. Each person gets **30 seconds** to share:

1. **Rate it:** Thumbs up, thumbs down, or sideways?
2. **In one sentence:** What's this book about? (Not the plot — the *idea*)

*Don't let this become a full discussion. The goal is to hear every voice early so quieter members feel included.*

## Discussion Questions

### Theme 1: Regret & the Roads Not Taken (7:15–7:30)

> *"Between life and death there is a library... and within that library, the shelves go on forever. Every book provides a chance to try another life you could have lived."*

**Question 1:** Nora's regrets include not marrying Dan, quitting swimming, not going to Australia with Izzy, and not continuing with her band. **Which of her regrets did you relate to the most, and why?**

*Facilitator note: Start with this because it's personal and accessible. Everyone has regrets. Let 3-4 people share before moving on.*

**Question 2:** Mrs. Elm tells Nora: "You don't have to understand life. You just have to live it." **Do you agree with that, or do you think understanding your life is part of living it well?**

**Question 3:** In almost every alternate life, Nora finds something wonderful AND something disappointing. She becomes an Olympic swimmer but is lonely. She's a glaciologist but is isolated in the Arctic. **Is Haig saying that every life has trade-offs, or is he saying something more specific about Nora?**

**Question 4:** Nora regrets breaking up with Dan, but in the life where they're married, she discovers he's been unfaithful. **Does this scene change how you think about relationship regrets in your own life? Is it comforting or disturbing to think that the "what if" version might not have been better?**

*Conversation rescue prompt (if discussion stalls): "Has anyone ever reconnected with someone from a 'road not taken' and found out the reality didn't match the fantasy?"*

---

### Theme 2: Depression, Despair & the Decision to Live (7:30–7:45)

> *"The only way to learn is to live."*

**⚠️ Facilitator note:** These questions touch on depression and suicidal ideation. Open this section by saying: *"The next few questions deal with some of the heavier themes in the book — depression and Nora's attempt to end her life. We want to discuss these respectfully. If anyone would rather skip this section, that's completely fine."*

**Question 5:** The book opens with Nora at her lowest point — she's lost her job, her cat has died, her brother doesn't return her calls. **Did you find the portrayal of depression realistic? If you've experienced depression (and only share if you're comfortable), did anything in Nora's internal monologue ring true?**

**Question 6:** Matt Haig has been open about his own struggles with depression and wrote *Reasons to Stay Alive* before this novel. **How does knowing the author's personal experience change how you read the book? Does it feel more authentic? More like a message he's trying to send?**

**Question 7:** Some readers have criticized the book for suggesting that depression can be "solved" by choosing to appreciate your life. **Is that a fair criticism? Or is the book more nuanced than that?**

*Facilitator note: This question often sparks the strongest debate. Let it breathe. If someone says the book oversimplifies mental illness, validate that perspective. If someone says it helped them, validate that too. Both can be true.*

**Question 8:** Nora's root life — the one she initially wanted to leave — turns out to be the one she fights to return to. **Why? What changed? Is it that her life actually got better, or that her perspective shifted?**

---

### Theme 3: Identity — Who Are You Across Infinite Lives? (7:45–8:00)

> *"She was alive, and she was herself, and it was enough."*

**Question 9:** In the life where Nora is a famous rock star, she has everything she thought she wanted — but she doesn't recognize herself. **Is there a "core you" that exists across all possible lives, or are we entirely shaped by our choices and circumstances?**

**Question 10:** Nora's relationship with her brother Hugo is different in every life. In some, they're close; in others, they're strangers. **What does this say about the nature of family relationships? Are they inherent, or do they require active maintenance?**

**Question 11:** Nora is a different person in each life — different skills, different relationships, different confidence levels. But she always retains her memories from her root life. **If you woke up tomorrow in a version of your life where you had made all different choices but still remembered THIS life, which version of "you" would feel most real?**

**Question 12:** Mrs. Elm appears in the Midnight Library as a guiding figure — Nora's childhood school librarian. **Why a librarian? What does the choice of a librarian (rather than a doctor, therapist, or religious figure) say about Haig's worldview?**

*Conversation rescue prompt: "Think about the people in your life who've been 'Mrs. Elms' — quiet presences who shaped you more than you realized at the time."*

---

### Theme 4: The Philosophy of a Good Life (8:00–8:15)

> *"Want is an interesting word. It means lack."*

**Question 13:** Nora eventually learns that no life is perfect, but some lives are more "right" for her than others. **How do you define a "good life"? Has your definition changed as you've gotten older?**

**Question 14:** The book implicitly argues that a life of connection (friends, love, community) matters more than achievement (fame, success, money). **Do you buy that? Or is that too convenient a conclusion?**

**Question 15:** Hugo, Nora's brother, appears in one life as someone who found peace through yoga and spirituality after their father's death. In another life, he's bitter and distant. **Does the book suggest that peace is a choice? Is that true, or is it a privilege?**

**Question 16:** Near the end, Nora realizes: "She didn't want to be fixed, she wanted to be found." **What's the difference between being fixed and being found? Which one have you experienced more in your own life?**

---

### Theme 5: Lightning Round — Fun & Debatable (8:15–8:25)

*Switch to a faster pace. Go around the room, one question at a time. Quick answers — no more than 30 seconds each.*

**Question 17:** **If you had access to the Midnight Library for one hour, which life would you try first?** (Not which you'd stay in — just which you'd peek at.)

**Question 18:** **Nora's cat, Voltaire, dies at the beginning of the book. Be honest: was the death of the cat more upsetting to you than Nora's suicide attempt?** (It's okay to say yes. Most people do.)

**Question 19:** **The Midnight Library is a metaphor. But for what, exactly?** Go around the room — each person gets one sentence. (Therapy? The brain? Purgatory? Imagination? Netflix?)

**Question 20:** **Rate this book 1–10 and tell us in exactly three words why.** Example: "8 — Hopeful but simplistic." "9 — Made me cry." "6 — Too neat ending."

---

## Closing Activity (8:25–8:30)

### "The Book I'd Write"

**Instructions:** Each person shares in one sentence: **"If the Midnight Library held a book about the life you'd live if you made one different choice, what would the title be?"**

Examples:
- "The One Where I Moved to Barcelona"
- "What If I Said Yes at the Airport"
- "The Life Where I Actually Practiced Piano"

*This sends people home with a smile and something to think about. It also tends to generate the most laughter of the evening.*

## Facilitator Tips

### If Discussion Dies Early
- **Redirect to the text:** "Let's look at a specific scene. Can someone find the passage where Nora first enters the library? What details stood out?"
- **Use "bridging" questions:** "That's interesting — does anyone see it differently?" or "How does that connect to what [name] said earlier?"
- **Ask for the opposition:** "We've heard a lot of positive reactions. Did anyone NOT like this book? What didn't work for you?" (This often generates the best discussion.)

### If One Person Dominates
- "Great point, [name]. I want to make sure we hear from everyone — [other name], what did you think about that?"
- Use the "popcorn" method: after answering, each person chooses who speaks next.

### If Things Get Too Personal
- With topics like depression, redirect to the text: "Thank you for sharing that. Going back to the book — how does Haig handle that same feeling on the page?"

### If Nobody Read the Book
- *(This happens.)* Focus on Questions 4, 9, 13, 14, 17, and 19 — these work even with minimal knowledge of the plot. The premise (a library of alternate lives) is enough context.
