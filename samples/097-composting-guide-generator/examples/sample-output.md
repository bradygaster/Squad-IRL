## 🌱 Why Vermicomposting Is Perfect for You

Mei-Lin, let's start with the good news: the kind of composting that works best in an apartment is also the kind that produces the *richest* fertilizer for your herb garden. It's called **vermicomposting** — composting with worms.

Here's why it's your best match:

| Your constraint | Why vermicomposting fits |
|---|---|
| No yard | A worm bin is roughly the size of a storage tote and lives on your balcony or under the kitchen sink |
| Small balcony (4' × 8') | The bin takes up about 2 square feet — less than 7% of your balcony |
| Vegetarian kitchen scraps | Worms *love* vegetable peels, coffee grounds, and eggshells — exactly what you produce |
| Smell concerns | A healthy worm bin smells like forest floor. Seriously. No rot, no stink. |
| $75 budget | Full setup costs $45–$60 |
| 5-year-old daughter | Kids are fascinated by worms. This becomes a science project, not a chore. |
| Portland climate | Portland's mild temperatures (avg. 40–80 °F) are in the ideal range for red wiggler worms year-round |
| Herb garden goal | Worm castings (worm poop) are often called "black gold" — they're one of the best fertilizers on the planet |

### Why Your Bucket Failed

Before we go further, let's diagnose what went wrong last time. You tossed scraps in a bucket and it turned moldy and smelly within a week. That happened because of three things:

1. **No aeration.** Decomposition needs oxygen. A sealed bucket goes anaerobic (no oxygen), and anaerobic bacteria produce hydrogen sulfide — that rotten-egg smell. Think swamp vs. forest floor.
2. **No carbon balance.** Food scraps are "greens" (nitrogen-rich). Without "browns" (carbon-rich material like shredded newspaper or dry leaves), the nitrogen breaks down into ammonia. That's the sharp, eye-watering smell.
3. **No drainage.** Decomposing food releases liquid. In a bucket with no holes, the scraps sit in their own leachate and ferment.

A worm bin solves all three problems by design: it has ventilation holes, a bedding layer of carbon-rich material, and a drainage system. The worms do the heavy lifting — they eat the scraps and excrete castings, skipping the smelly rot phase entirely.

## 🪱 Choosing Your Worms

Not just any earthworm will do. You need **red wigglers** (*Eisenia fetida*), sometimes sold as "red worms" or "manure worms." Here's why:

| Trait | Red wigglers | Garden earthworms |
|---|---|---|
| Live in top few inches of soil/material | ✅ Yes | ❌ No — they burrow deep |
| Eat half their body weight in food per day | ✅ Yes | ❌ Much less |
| Reproduce quickly in bins | ✅ Yes | ❌ Rarely |
| Tolerate bin conditions | ✅ Yes | ❌ They'll try to escape |

### How Many Worms?

The rule of thumb: **1 pound of worms per ½ pound of daily food waste**. Most households produce about ½ lb of compostable scraps per day, so 1 pound (≈ 1,000 worms) is ideal. But starting with ½ pound (≈ 500 worms) is fine — they'll double in population within 2–3 months if conditions are good.

Start with ½ pound. They'll catch up.

## 🔨 Setting Up Your Worm Bin

### Step 1: Prepare the Totes

Take one 10-gallon Rubbermaid tote. This will be the **working bin** (where the worms live).

**Drill ventilation holes:**
- **Lid:** Drill 20–30 small holes (1/16" bit) across the lid for airflow
- **Upper sides:** Drill two rows of 1/16" holes near the top of each long side (about 10 holes per side)
- **Bottom:** Drill 15–20 holes (¼" bit) in the bottom for drainage

Take the second tote. This is the **catch tray**. Do **not** drill any holes in it. It sits underneath to collect excess moisture (this liquid is "worm tea" — dilute it 10:1 with water and use it as fertilizer).

**Stack them:** Set small blocks, bottle caps, or pieces of wood (about 1" tall) inside the catch tray, then rest the working bin on top. This creates an air gap so the drainage holes don't sit in a puddle.

```
┌──────────────────────────────┐
│          LID (with holes)    │  ← airflow
├──────────────────────────────┤
│                              │
│    WORKING BIN               │  ← worms, bedding, food
│    (holes in sides, bottom)  │
│                              │
├──────┬──────────────┬────────┤
│      │  air gap     │        │  ← spacer blocks
├──────┴──────────────┴────────┤
│    CATCH TRAY (no holes)     │  ← collects worm tea
└──────────────────────────────┘
```

**Cover the bottom holes** of the working bin with a single layer of window screen mesh or landscape fabric. This lets liquid drain through but keeps worms and bedding in.

### Step 2: Prepare the Bedding

The bedding is where the worms live between meals. It should be:
- **Carbon-rich** (shredded newspaper, cardboard, or coconut coir)
- **Moist but not wet** — like a wrung-out sponge (about 60–70% moisture)
- **Fluffy** — worms need air pockets to breathe through their skin

**To prepare newspaper bedding:**
1. Tear newspaper into strips about 1" wide and 6–8" long. Avoid glossy inserts.
2. Soak the strips in water for 5 minutes.
3. Squeeze out excess water. Each handful should feel like a wrung-out sponge — damp, not dripping.
4. Fluff the strips and fill the working bin about 4–6 inches deep.

**If using coconut coir:**
1. Place the coir brick in a bucket, add warm water, and let it expand (one brick makes about 8 quarts of bedding).
2. Squeeze out excess water to the wrung-out sponge consistency.
3. Mix 50/50 with shredded newspaper for the best texture.

Sprinkle two handfuls of clean garden soil or finished compost on top. This introduces beneficial microorganisms and gives the worms grit for their gizzards (worms have no teeth — they use grit to grind food).

### Step 3: Add the Worms

Dump the worms onto the surface of the bedding. Leave the lid off and a light on above the bin for 15–20 minutes. Red wigglers are photophobic (they hate light) and will burrow down into the bedding to escape it. This prevents them from trying to explore over the edges.

### Step 4: First Feeding (Wait 3 Days)

Let the worms settle in for **3 full days** before feeding. They need time to acclimate to their new home. The bedding itself provides some food in the meantime.

### Step 5: Placement

**Best spot: your covered balcony.**

Portland's average temperatures are ideal for red wigglers:
- Spring/summer: 55–80 °F → perfect
- Fall: 45–60 °F → fine
- Winter: 35–45 °F → worms slow down but survive; add insulation (see Seasonal Tips)

Place the bin in a **shaded corner** of the balcony, out of direct sun. Direct sunlight can overheat the bin in summer. The covered balcony protects against Portland's rain, which would flood an open bin.

**Alternative: under the kitchen sink.** If winter temperatures drop below freezing for extended periods, move the bin indoors. A healthy worm bin has no smell — seriously.

## ♻️ What to Compost

As a vegetarian cook, almost everything from your kitchen prep is worm food. The key concept is the **carbon-to-nitrogen ratio (C:N ratio)**. Worms and their microbial helpers work best at a C:N ratio of about **25:1 to 30:1** by weight.

### 🟢 "Greens" — Nitrogen-Rich (C:N ≈ 15–25:1)

These are your food scraps. They provide protein and nitrogen to fuel microbial activity.

| Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| Vegetable peels & scraps | Chop large pieces into 1–2" chunks for faster breakdown |
| Fruit scraps & peels | Banana peels, apple cores, melon rinds — all good. Cut citrus in small pieces (see note below) |
| Coffee grounds & filters | Excellent. Worms love coffee grounds. Paper filters are fine. |
| Tea bags (paper only) | Remove staples. Rip open bags so worms can reach the leaves. |
| Eggshells | Crush finely — they don't break down fast but add calcium and grit. Rinse first. |
| Cooked plain grains (rice, pasta) | In small amounts. No oil or sauce. |
| Stale bread | Tear into small pieces. Moisten if very dry. |
| Wilted herbs or lettuce | Your garden trimmings go right back in |
| Houseplant trimmings | Non-toxic plants only |

**Citrus note:** Small amounts of lemon, lime, or orange peel are fine. Large amounts make the bin too acidic (worms prefer pH 6.0–7.0). Limit citrus to about 10% of total food input.

### 🟤 "Browns" — Carbon-Rich (C:N ≈ 50–200:1)

These absorb moisture, prevent odor, and balance the nitrogen from food scraps.

| Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| Shredded newspaper | Your primary brown. Non-glossy pages only. |
| Cardboard (torn into small pieces) | Cereal boxes, egg cartons, toilet paper rolls — remove any tape or plastic windows |
| Paper towels & napkins (unbleached) | Only if they touched food, not cleaning chemicals |
| Dry leaves | Portland gives you plenty every fall. Store a bag for year-round use. |
| Coconut coir | Excellent moisture regulator. Buy one brick — it lasts months. |
| Dryer lint (from 100% cotton/natural fiber loads) | Small amounts only |
| Sawdust (untreated wood) | Very carbon-rich — use sparingly or it locks up nitrogen |

### The Ratio in Practice

You don't need a scale. Use the **handful rule**: every time you add a handful of food scraps, add **two handfuls of shredded newspaper or cardboard** on top. This approximates the 25–30:1 C:N ratio and also serves as a cover to prevent fruit flies.

## ❌ What NEVER to Add

| Item | Why |
|---|---|
| Meat, fish, or bones | Attracts pests, produces foul odor as it decays, can introduce harmful pathogens |
| Dairy products (cheese, yogurt, butter) | Same as meat — rot, smell, pests |
| Oils, fats, or greasy food | Coats worm skin and suffocates them; worms breathe through their skin |
| Pet waste (dog/cat feces) | Contains parasites and pathogens dangerous to humans |
| Diseased plants | Can spread pathogens into your compost and then into your herb garden |
| Treated/pressure-treated wood | Contains arsenic, chromium, copper — toxic to worms and your food garden |
| Glossy or coated paper | Plastic coatings don't break down; may contain toxins |
| Cleaning product residues | Chemicals kill worms and beneficial microbes |
| Large amounts of citrus or onion | Highly acidic; small amounts are okay, large amounts crash the pH and kill worms |
| Spicy peppers (habanero, jalapeño) | Capsaicin irritates worms. Mild peppers like bell peppers are fine. |

## 📅 Weekly Maintenance Routine

Vermicomposting is remarkably low-effort. Here's your weekly schedule:

### Monday: Feed (3 minutes)

1. Pull back the top layer of bedding in one corner of the bin.
2. Add your collected food scraps from the past 3–4 days.
3. Cover the scraps with 2–3 handfuls of shredded newspaper.
4. Replace the lid.

**Rotate feeding corners** each time (front-left → front-right → back-right → back-left). This gives worms time to finish each deposit and prevents overloading one area.

### Thursday: Feed again (3 minutes)

Repeat the Monday process in the next corner.

### Sunday: Quick health check (2 minutes)

1. **Lift the lid and sniff.** Earthy smell = good. Sour or rotten smell = problem (see Troubleshooting).
2. **Check moisture.** Grab a handful of bedding and squeeze. A few drops of water should come out. If it's dry, mist with the spray bottle. If it's soggy, add dry shredded newspaper.
3. **Peek at the worms.** They should be active and distributed through the top few inches. If they're all clustered at the surface or trying to escape, something is wrong.
4. **Check the catch tray.** If there's liquid, pour it into a jar. Dilute 10:1 with water and use it to water your herbs. This is worm tea — it's nutrient-rich liquid fertilizer.

**Total weekly time: ~8 minutes.**

## 👃 Smell Prevention

This is your top concern, so let's be thorough. A properly managed worm bin does **not** smell. Here is every scenario that causes odor and how to prevent or fix it:

### The Science of Smell in Composting

Odor in decomposition comes from **anaerobic bacteria** — microbes that thrive without oxygen. They produce:
- **Hydrogen sulfide** (H₂S) — rotten egg smell
- **Ammonia** (NH₃) — sharp, eye-watering smell
- **Volatile fatty acids** — sour, vinegar-like smell

Aerobic bacteria (the ones you want) produce **carbon dioxide and water** — both odorless. The entire design of a worm bin is to keep things aerobic.

### Five Smell-Prevention Rules

1. **Always bury food under bedding.** Exposed food attracts fruit flies and begins to rot on the surface. Every feeding should end with a 2-inch layer of newspaper on top.

2. **Don't overfeed.** Worms eat roughly half their body weight per day. For ½ lb of worms, that's ¼ lb of food per day (about 1.5–2 lbs per week). If food is piling up uneaten, cut back.

3. **Maintain the C:N ratio.** Two handfuls of browns for every one handful of greens. If you smell ammonia, add more browns immediately.

4. **Keep moisture right.** Too wet = anaerobic pockets. Too dry = worms stop eating and food rots instead. Aim for that wrung-out sponge feel (60–70% moisture).

5. **Ensure airflow.** Don't block the ventilation holes. Don't pack bedding down tightly. Fluff it gently every few weeks.

### Why Your Bucket Failed — The Full Explanation

Your bucket had zero of these five elements:

| Smell-prevention factor | Your bucket | Worm bin |
|---|---|---|
| Buried food | ❌ Scraps sat exposed | ✅ Covered with bedding every time |
| Portion control | ❌ Everything dumped at once | ✅ Fed in small amounts, 2×/week |
| C:N balance | ❌ All nitrogen, no carbon | ✅ Newspaper/cardboard added with every feeding |
| Moisture control | ❌ No drainage, scraps sat in liquid | ✅ Drainage holes, catch tray, absorbent bedding |
| Airflow | ❌ Sealed bucket, no holes | ✅ Ventilation holes in lid and sides |

Your bucket was essentially a miniature swamp. The worm bin is a miniature forest floor. Completely different ecosystems.

## 🐛 Troubleshooting Guide

### Problem 1: Bin smells sour or like vinegar

**Cause:** Overfeeding or too much moisture. Food is fermenting anaerobically.
**Fix:**
- Stop feeding for 1 week.
- Add a thick layer (3–4 inches) of dry shredded newspaper or cardboard.
- Gently fluff the bedding to introduce air.
- Leave the lid cracked open for a few hours (with light on, so worms stay in).

### Problem 2: Bin smells like ammonia

**Cause:** Too much nitrogen (greens) relative to carbon (browns).
**Fix:**
- Add a large amount of shredded newspaper or cardboard immediately.
- Reduce greens at next feeding and increase browns.
- Check that you're following the 2:1 browns-to-greens handful rule.

### Problem 3: Fruit flies

**Cause:** Exposed food scraps on the surface.
**Fix:**
- Always bury food under 2+ inches of bedding.
- Freeze scraps for 24 hours before adding — this kills fruit fly eggs that may be on banana peels, etc.
- Place a piece of damp newspaper flat across the entire surface as a "blanket."
- If flies are already present, set a trap: a jar with apple cider vinegar and a drop of dish soap, covered with plastic wrap with small holes poked in it.

### Problem 4: Worms are trying to escape (crawling up walls or onto lid)

**Cause:** Bin conditions are hostile — usually too wet, too acidic, too hot, or too much fresh food.
**Fix:**
- Check moisture (too wet? add dry bedding).
- Check for overfeeding (uneaten food rotting? stop feeding for a week).
- Check pH — if you've added a lot of citrus or onion, add a tablespoon of crushed eggshells to buffer acidity.
- Keep a light on above the bin for 24–48 hours. Worms will stay in the bedding to avoid light.

### Problem 5: Worms are dying

**Cause:** Could be temperature extremes, chemical contamination, or severely wrong conditions.
**Fix:**
- Check temperature: red wigglers die below 32 °F and above 95 °F. Move the bin indoors if the balcony is too hot or too cold.
- Did you add any chemical-contaminated material (cleaning wipes, treated wood, etc.)? Remove it immediately.
- Is the bin bone-dry or waterlogged? Correct moisture levels.
- Are there signs of pests (centipedes, ants)? See Problem 9.

### Problem 6: Food is not breaking down

**Cause:** Pieces are too large, bin is too cold, or not enough worms yet.
**Fix:**
- Chop food into smaller pieces (1–2 inches). The smaller the piece, the more surface area for microbes and worms.
- If it's winter and the bin is on the balcony, move it indoors or insulate it (see Seasonal Tips).
- If you started with ½ lb of worms, be patient. They'll double in 2–3 months and processing speed will increase.

### Problem 7: Bin is too wet / waterlogged

**Cause:** Too many high-moisture scraps (watermelon, cucumber, tomato) without enough browns.
**Fix:**
- Add a large amount of dry shredded newspaper or cardboard.
- Leave the lid cracked for a few hours to let some moisture evaporate (with a light on).
- Drain the catch tray.
- Going forward, mix in more browns whenever you add watery scraps.

### Problem 8: Bin is too dry

**Cause:** Too much dry bedding, not enough food, or dry air (less common in Portland).
**Fix:**
- Mist the bedding with your spray bottle until it reaches wrung-out sponge moisture.
- Add more food scraps (they carry moisture).
- In Portland's dry August weeks, check moisture more frequently.

### Problem 9: Ants in the bin

**Cause:** Bin is too dry or has exposed sweet food (fruit).
**Fix:**
- Increase moisture — ants dislike damp conditions.
- Bury all food under bedding.
- If the bin is on the balcony, set each leg of the stand in a shallow dish of water (moat method) to block ant trails.

### Problem 10: White mold on food scraps

**Cause:** Normal. White mold is a sign of fungal decomposition — it's part of the process.
**Fix:**
- No action needed. The worms and other organisms will consume it.
- If it bothers you, bury it under a layer of bedding.
- White mold is *not* the same as the mold in your old bucket. In the bucket, mold was the *only* decomposer because there was no oxygen for anything else. In the worm bin, it's one player among many.

### Problem 11: Tiny white worms/mites

**Cause:** Pot worms (enchytraeids) or mites. Both appear when the bin is slightly too acidic or too wet.
**Fix:**
- They're harmless and won't hurt your red wigglers.
- To reduce them: cut back on acidic scraps (citrus, tomatoes), add crushed eggshells, and reduce moisture slightly.

### Problem 12: Bin is attracting other pests (mice, raccoons)

**Cause:** On a balcony, critters may investigate.
**Fix:**
- Ensure the lid fits tightly.
- Don't leave food exposed on the surface.
- The ventilation holes should be small enough (1/16") that nothing can enter.
- If raccoons are persistent, place a bungee cord around the bin to secure the lid, or weight the lid with a brick.

## 🌿 Using Your Compost

### What You're Making: Worm Castings

Worm castings are not regular compost. They're better. Here's the science:

| Property | Regular compost | Worm castings |
|---|---|---|
| Nitrogen (N) | 1–2% | 2–3% |
| Phosphorus (P) | 0.5–1% | 1.5–2.5% |
| Potassium (K) | 0.5–1% | 1–1.5% |
| Beneficial microbes | Moderate | Extremely high — 10–20× more than regular compost |
| Water retention | Good | Excellent — castings hold 2–3× their weight in water |
| Plant-available nutrients | Moderate (slow release) | High (humic acid makes nutrients immediately available) |
| pH | Variable | Near-neutral (6.5–7.0), perfect for most herbs |

### How to Harvest Castings

When the bin contents are dark, crumbly, and earthy-smelling (no recognizable food scraps), it's time to harvest. This takes about 3–4 months after setup.

**The Light Method (easiest):**
1. Push all bin contents to one side.
2. Fill the empty side with fresh, moist bedding and start feeding only on that side.
3. Over 2–3 weeks, worms will migrate to the food side.
4. Scoop out the worm-free castings from the old side.

**The Dump-and-Sort Method (faster, fun for kids):**
1. Spread the bin contents on a tarp or large sheet of plastic in sunlight.
2. Shape it into several small cone-shaped piles.
3. Wait 15–20 minutes. Worms will burrow to the bottom of each pile to escape light.
4. Scrape the outer layer of castings off each pile.
5. Wait another 10 minutes, scrape again. Repeat until you're left with a small cluster of worms at the base of each pile.
6. Return worms to the bin with fresh bedding.

### Using Castings on Your Herb Garden

| Use | How |
|---|---|
| Potting mix amendment | Mix castings 1:4 with regular potting soil. Use for repotting herbs. |
| Top dressing | Spread ½ inch of castings on the surface of each pot every 4–6 weeks. Water in. |
| Seed starting | Mix castings 1:3 with coconut coir for a nutrient-rich seed-starting mix. |
| Worm tea (liquid fertilizer) | Soak 1 cup of castings in 1 gallon of water for 24 hours, stirring occasionally. Strain and use to water plants. |

## 🌡️ Seasonal Tips for Portland

Portland's climate is one of the best in the US for vermicomposting. But each season has its nuances.

### 🌸 Spring (March–May)

- Worms become more active as temperatures rise. Increase feeding gradually.
- This is the best time to harvest castings and start your herb garden.
- Portland's spring rain won't affect a covered balcony bin, but check that wind-driven rain isn't soaking the bin.

### ☀️ Summer (June–August)

- **Heat is your biggest risk.** Portland can hit 90–100 °F in July and August.
- Move the bin to the shadiest part of the balcony.
- If temps exceed 85 °F for several consecutive days, move the bin indoors temporarily.
- Freeze a water bottle and place it in the bin as a cooling element on extreme heat days.
- Check moisture more often — summer heat evaporates water faster.

### 🍂 Fall (September–November)

- Ideal conditions. Worms are at peak productivity.
- Collect fallen leaves from neighborhood trees (Portland has abundant maples, oaks, and alders). Dry them and store in a bag — they're excellent browns and will last you all winter.
- Harvest castings before winter to top-dress your perennial herbs.

### ❄️ Winter (December–February)

- Portland rarely drops below freezing, but it happens.
- Below 40 °F, worms slow down significantly. Feed less — maybe once per week.
- Below 32 °F, worms can die. Take precautions:
  - **Insulate the bin:** Wrap it in an old blanket, bubble wrap, or a sleeping bag. Decomposition generates some heat internally.
  - **Move indoors:** Under the kitchen sink or in a closet. A healthy bin is odorless — your partner won't object.
  - **Add a thick bedding layer:** 6–8 inches of newspaper provides insulation from above.
- During Portland's typical winter (lows around 35–40 °F), an insulated bin on a covered balcony is usually fine. The worms just work slower.
